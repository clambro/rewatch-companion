The essay argues that humiliation in _Succession_ is not just cruelty but a system of instruction, one that lowers people in public, turns the lowering into shared memory, and converts disgrace into usable knowledge. Logan’s power rests on two forms of this pedagogy, the theatrical public shaming of “boar on the floor” and the colder report-card judgment that fixes each child in a permanent role, culminating in “you are not serious people” as a teacherly dismissal. Tom and Greg become the series’ clearest inheritors of this method because Tom learns to translate embarrassment into management while Greg learns to treat being mocked as a condition of access. Their scenes show humiliation becoming administrative, from the deck shoes insult to the wedding-morning slap, the “Safe Room” water-bottle attack, and the cruise-documents blackmail, all of which reveal a relationship that is less bullying than apprenticeship in downward force. The key claim is that Logan’s cruelties outlive him because they have been internalized as a portable corporate language: Tom can now administer the lesson with stickers, puns, and “executive-level” tantrums, while Greg can survive by turning abasement into leverage. By contrast, the Roy children remain trapped in humiliation as verdict and wound, unable to use it cleanly because they still want recognition from the father who issued the mark.
