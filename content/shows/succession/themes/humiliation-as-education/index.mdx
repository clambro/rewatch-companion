## Humiliation as Instruction

Humiliation in _Succession_ is one of the show’s teaching methods. A person is lowered, the lowering is witnessed or recorded, and the scene leaves behind a usable fact. Someone now knows where he stands. Someone else has learned how to place him there again.

The series gives humiliation three related forms. Logan Roy favors the public ritual, the staged reduction of adults into objects of spectacle. He also uses the report-card judgment, a blunt sentence that pretends to summarize a person once and for all. Around him, especially through Tom and Greg, humiliation becomes a transferable skill: learned, copied, refined, and passed downward in smaller rooms. That third form keeps the method alive after Logan’s body is gone.

“Boar on the floor” remains the most explicit version of the first form because it strips the method to its mechanics. Logan suspects disloyalty during the retreat in “Hunting,” gathers executives and dependents into a room, and turns inquiry into theater. Karl, Tom, and Greg crawl, grunt, and compete for sausages while the others watch. The game does not produce meaningful evidence. Its purpose is to arrange bodies into a picture of subjection. Logan does not need to prove who leaked. He needs the room to see who can be made to accept the terms of his suspicion.

The witnesses complete the act. Humiliation becomes durable when it enters group memory. The crawling, the animal noises, the tossed meat, the forced laughter, all remain ready for later use without requiring repetition. The room learns who can be lowered and who must pretend the lowering still belongs to the game.

The report-card form is colder. Logan often speaks as if he has finished assessing a person and now merely announces the grade. Kendall is weak until he is briefly useful as a killer. Roman is funny, dirty, unsteady, and therefore suspect. Shiv is clever enough to invite and undisciplined enough to deny. Connor is irrelevant while remaining near enough to bruise. Tom is serviceable because he can be used. Greg is ridiculous, which gives Tom something to work with.

“You are not serious people,” in “Rehearsal,” gives this habit its cleanest late form. Logan says it to the children after years of tests, promotions withdrawn, staged reconciliations, and baited promises. The line works because it does not argue. It places them. They have money, advisers, plans, and grievance, but he refuses them the category they want most. He speaks like a teacher returning an exam he has no intention of explaining. Their fury comes partly from recognizing the old classroom. They have spent their lives trying to earn a mark from someone who benefits from withholding it.

## The First Lessons Tom Gives Greg

Tom Wambsgans understands humiliation before he knows how to use it well. He enters the Roy world exposed on both sides: above him, the family can mock, test, or discard him; below him, almost no one is there for him to command. Greg supplies the opening. From their first office scenes, Tom’s treatment of him is less a side joke than an apprenticeship in miniature.

The deck-shoes scene establishes the pattern with unusual economy. Tom sees Greg’s footwear and turns sartorial contempt into managerial action. The mockery begins as social correction: Greg has arrived in the wrong costume, wearing shoes that, in Tom’s imagination, belong on the “poop deck” of a “majestic schooner.” But Tom does not leave the insult as insult. He immediately folds it into placement, asking where they might put “the talented Mr. Greg.” Condescension and recruitment blur. Tom lowers Greg, then hires the lowered version.

Tom’s authority over Greg does not begin with competence. It begins with ridicule made administrative. He identifies Greg as absurd, then treats absurdity as a reason to manage him. Greg receives two lessons at once: his embarrassment can mark him as inferior, and that same mark can get him through the door. The scene is comic because Tom’s theatrical disgust exceeds the shoes. It is exact because the excess has a purpose. Tom is practicing the Roy habit of turning social judgment into position.

Greg, for his part, starts as someone who feels embarrassment too visibly. He is tall, uncertain, badly timed, and almost always half a beat behind the room. Yet he learns quickly that shame does not expel him if he remains useful after it. He can be mocked and still placed. He can be threatened and still consulted. He can be treated as debris and still carry information no one else has.

Tom’s wedding morning assault in “Pre-Nuptial” sharpens the pattern. Greg tells Tom that Shiv is cheating. The information wounds Tom at the moment when he is supposed to be most publicly secured by marriage. He cannot strike Shiv, cannot confront the Roy family, and cannot admit what the knowledge does to him. So he turns on Greg. He covers Greg’s mouth, slaps him, and drives him down, trying to force the fact back into the person who delivered it.

The scene appears before the cruise hearings, before prison anxiety, before Tom’s more explicit corporate education in sacrifice. Tom is already converting hurt into downward force. He has not yet mastered the method, and the outburst is clumsy, almost panicked. But the direction is fixed. Pain arrives from above or beside him, and he discharges it onto the one person with less protection. Greg becomes a subordinate not only in the org chart but in Tom’s emotional hydraulics.

Their relationship is a training arrangement as much as a comic dependency. Tom teaches Greg how to be humiliated and remain near advantage. Greg teaches Tom that humiliation administered downward can create attachment as well as fear. Each needs the other to make the lesson repeatable.

## Safe Rooms and Paper Trails

The “Safe Room” episode turns the Tom-Greg apprenticeship into a closed experiment. The building is in alarm, executives have been moved into protected spaces, and Tom finds himself trapped with Greg at the precise moment Greg tries to renegotiate their arrangement. Greg proposes leaving ATN while maintaining something like a “business open relationship.” The phrase lands because it repeats, in corporate miniature, the wound Shiv has already given Tom in marriage.

Tom’s reaction is immediate and physical. He pelts Greg with water bottles, turning the room that promises protection into another chamber of instruction. The violence is small enough to remain comic and sharp enough to reveal the transfer. Tom cannot control Shiv’s terms. He can control Greg’s body in the room. He calls the outburst “executive-level business,” a phrase that tries to dignify tantrum as management. The joke exposes the aspiration. Tom wants his injury to look like command.

Greg’s later move in the same episode shows that he has been learning. He admits he did not burn all the cruise documents and floats the possibility of using them against Tom. The blackmail is tentative, ungainly, and full of Greg’s usual verbal fog, but Tom recognizes the development. His approving “Atta boy” response acknowledges that humiliation has become instruction. Greg has taken the conditions of his abasement and found leverage inside them.

Tom’s approval is not benevolence. He is pleased because Greg has begun to speak the local language. The student has learned that evidence can answer insult, that being used may produce material, that survival requires keeping something back.

The Senate hearing in “DC” gives this private curriculum a public record. “You can’t make a Tomlette without breaking some Greggs” arrives as an email subject line read into a formal proceeding. It is juvenile, self-amused, and revealing. Tom casts himself as the dish being made and Greg as the eggs to be broken. Advancement requires damage, and the damage will be borne by the subordinate whose name can be turned into a pun.

The line joins all three forms of humiliation. It is a joke from a superior to a subordinate, preserved as institutional evidence, repeated before an audience with microphones and legal stakes. The hearing room does not create the humiliation; it changes its scale and texture. What had seemed like office banter now reads as a written trace of method.

Tom’s mistake is not that he has learned the lesson. His mistake is that he has left the lesson captioned. Logan’s cruelties often survive as memory, atmosphere, and fear. Tom’s survives in email. The phrase shows both his aptitude and his amateurism. He understands that Greg can be broken for Tom’s benefit. He has not yet learned to keep the breaking deniable.

## The Roy Children Cannot Use the Lesson Cleanly

The Roy children know humiliation more intimately than anyone, but they do not convert it into a stable craft. Their father’s judgments remain too bound up with desire. Each child tries to reverse the mark, disprove it, or seize the position from which marks are given. That effort keeps them near the original wound.

Kendall repeatedly stages his own exposure as if performance could master it. The failed vote of no confidence in “Which Side Are You On?” teaches him that Logan’s physical presence can bend procedure back into family fear. His press conference at the end of season two briefly reverses the direction of shame, turning the cruises sacrifice into an accusation against Logan. Yet even that victory depends on Logan’s vocabulary. Kendall acts as the “killer” he was told he was not. He wins the moment by accepting the terms of the insult and trying to inhabit its opposite.

His later gestures become less usable. The Nirvana stunt against Shiv in “The Disruption” tries to answer humiliation with humiliation, but the act is too theatrical to create authority. His birthday party in “Too Much Birthday” builds a whole environment out of self-display and leaves him stranded inside it. “Living+” gives him a cleaner version of command, one presentation in which ridicule briefly turns into momentum, but the success remains fragile because it still seeks applause where Logan would have settled for obedience.

Roman handles humiliation by moving toward it before others can push him. He makes himself obscene, unserious, and open to insult, which lets him survive many scenes without appearing to ask for anything. The strategy fails when he needs recognition without disguise. At Logan’s funeral, language deserts him. In the final boardroom struggle, his “we are bullshit” clarity arrives too late to become action. Roman can name the falseness of the ritual, but he cannot step outside its effects.

Shiv’s defense is distance. She tries to hear the insult before it lands and answer from a position of irony or competence. Logan repeatedly defeats that defense by offering her proximity and then withdrawing it. Her intelligence does not protect her from the exam because she keeps returning to the examiner. In the finale, her refusal to crown Kendall is partly strategic, partly instinctive, partly an answer to the old classroom. She will not give him the grade their father withheld from all of them.

The children can wound one another with precision, but their use of humiliation remains tangled with the wish to be recognized by Logan or by the office he occupied. They administer the lesson as heirs to a family drama. Tom and Greg learn it as a working condition.

## The Method After Logan

Tom’s eventual rise depends on his ability to endure degradation without treating it as a final description of himself. The Roy children hear humiliation as a verdict from the father. Tom hears it as weather, painful, dangerous, but navigable. Shiv’s sexual and marital cruelties hurt him. Logan’s willingness to sacrifice him during cruises frightens him. The family’s jokes diminish him. He absorbs these injuries, resents them, and looks for a place to send them next.

His rare upward gestures feel unstable for this reason. Eating Logan’s chicken on the yacht is funny because it is absurdly intimate and briefly fearless. But Tom’s lasting technique runs downward and later outward. He learns to present compliance upward while administering pressure below. By the time Matsson chooses him, Tom’s value lies in that combination: he can take insult, keep functioning, and make unpleasant decisions without requiring the dignity of ownership.

Greg’s final position shows the same education from the other side. He becomes almost serene in his capacity to be marked. He can betray, be exposed, be struck, be retained, and continue hovering near the table. In the finale, after Greg’s attempted information trade is discovered, Tom places a sticker on his forehead. The gesture is childish, proprietary, and efficient. It does not require the theatrical machinery of “boar on the floor.” Greg accepts the mark because the mark comes with access.

The sticker is a late refinement of the same grammar. Logan needed rooms to witness abasement and sentences to grade it. Tom can do it with office supplies. Greg does not need the meaning explained. He has spent the series learning that ridicule may be the price of continued proximity, and that proximity can matter more than pride.

Logan’s death does not end the pedagogy. It removes the central examiner and redistributes the right to examine. The siblings keep using humiliation as an inheritance they cannot control, turning the final boardroom fight into mutual exposure. Tom and Greg remain because they have learned the more portable version. One can administer the lesson in the language of business necessity. The other can survive it long enough for survival to look like adaptability.
