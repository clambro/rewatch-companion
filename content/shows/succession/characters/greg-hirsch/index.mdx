Greg Hirsch enters _Succession_ inside Waystar Royco before he understands what Waystar Royco is. The pilot’s mascot suit is more than an introductory humiliation. It places him literally inside the company’s costume, made to perform its friendliness for children while his body revolts against the job. Vomit leaks through the eye holes, and the image is exact: Greg is already looking out from within the brand, sickened by it, implicated in it, and unable to stop watching.

The scene refuses the comfort of innocence. Greg has smoked weed before work, lost control of a basic assignment, and turned the company’s cheerful face into a public mess. His failure is absurd, but it is also useful. Fired from the training program, he moves toward the Roys through the only credential he has, a diluted blood claim. He has no authority, no competence, and no developed plan. He has proximity. That is enough to get him through the first door.

That first position never really changes. Greg remains slightly outside the main contest and strangely open to it. He enters rooms with the wrong face on. He asks questions a second too late. He misunderstands tone and looks around after everyone else has understood what the conversation is really about. Yet the same awkwardness makes him a recording device. People speak near him because they do not quite count him. They treat him as furniture with nerves.

The pilot also gives him the method he will keep refining. Greg learns by being corrected, mocked, renamed, threatened, and then allowed to remain. When Logan calls him by the wrong name and Greg offers to answer to both, the line is more than meek. It is adaptive. He will accept misrecognition if it keeps him near the table. He would like respect, but he does not require it as a condition of participation.

His recurring motion is small but consistent: a pause, a nervous question, a little ethical squirm, then compliance once the nearest authority makes the request feel survivable. Greg’s shame turns useful. If he can endure embarrassment without leaving, he can stay long enough to learn what it buys.

The show never turns this into clean advancement. Greg does not become fluent. He hardens selectively. He grows more willing to use what he knows, more willing to let someone else suffer from his timing, more willing to pretend that uncertainty absolves him. But he keeps looking provisional. Even when he matters, he looks as if he has wandered into consequence a few minutes after consequence began.

## Tom’s School of Humiliation

Tom Wambsgans gives Greg his real education. The Roys insult Greg intermittently, often as a reflex. Tom makes humiliation into curriculum. He receives contempt from above and translates it into exercises, tests, lectures, and little ceremonies of rank. Greg serves as his assistant, pet, confidant, witness, and practice subject.

Their scenes work because Tom’s cruelty has shape. He does more than mock Greg. He stages lessons. A restaurant turns into a status exam. A suit stands as evidence of weakness. A document becomes a thing to destroy and also a thing one might keep. Tom teaches through menace, but also through explanation. He wants Greg to understand the rule while feeling the rule press against his face.

This instruction carries resentment in both directions. Tom needs someone below him because the Roys never let him forget his own conditional status. Greg needs someone above him because he has entered a building whose language he cannot speak. Each man uses the other to stabilize a place he has not earned in the Roy sense.

The cruelty is practical. Tom shows Greg that closeness can be maintained through alternating favor and threat. Greg learns that intimacy in this environment rarely arrives without a hook in it. Tom can make Greg feel chosen and disposable in the same exchange.

The cruises documents give Greg his first serious opportunity to apply it. Tom orders him into the destruction of evidence, and Greg obeys visibly while disobeying privately. He shreds enough to count as compliant. He saves enough to matter later. The action is not bold. It is furtive, frightened, and half-comic. But it proves that Greg has learned the basic grammar: do what the stronger man asks, but keep a piece of the act for yourself if you can hide it.

From that point, Tom and Greg form a loop of apprenticeship and resentment. Tom pushes Greg down, then depends on the alertness that pressure produces. Greg absorbs Tom’s methods, then turns them into small acts of leverage. Tom wants Greg permanently below him, but his own teaching makes that impossible. A good subordinate in this world must learn to betray in miniature. Greg does.

Their season-three confrontations sharpen this. When Tom tries to provoke him, looming, needling, turning aggression into a grotesque office game, Greg does not suddenly become brave. He simply stops being the same soft target for a minute. The refusal is awkward and physical, almost ungainly. Tom sees that Greg has learned enough to resist without leaving the relationship. He has not escaped Tom’s school. He has become one of its products.

## Evidence, Delay, and the Nearest Authority

Greg’s saved papers alter his position. He is no longer only the tall cousin who says the wrong thing. He is a witness with an archive. The series does not remake him into a strategist. Greg rarely sees the whole board. He often misreads the room’s speed. What he learns is narrower and more useful: keep something back, wait, and spend it only when the buyer is strong.

His conscience has a particular shape. It slows him down without stopping him. With the cruises material, he understands enough to know that destruction of evidence is wrong. That recognition produces hesitations, procedural questions, and nervous jokes. It does not produce refusal. He wants someone else to convert the unacceptable into office procedure.

That habit makes him effective because it keeps him movable. He can tell himself he has not fully chosen while continuing to choose. His innocence becomes less a fact than a posture he can return to when useful. He never loses the look of a man startled by his own conduct.

Season three tests the posture under sharper pressure. Kendall’s rebellion offers Greg a flattering version of hesitation. He can stand near exposure of the cruises scandal, enjoy the aesthetics of righteousness, attend the better parties, and imagine that his usefulness has a moral glow. His “sturdy birdy” pledge is funny because the phrase cannot bear the decision it advertises. Greg wants commitment to sound safe before it is safe.

Logan’s presence resolves the problem in the way Greg has been trained to accept. He comes close, drinks what he is given, shakes visibly, and tries to match the old man’s appetite for risk without possessing any of its ease. It is not a heroic correction or a principled defection. It is the least bad choice as Greg reads it. Kendall can flatter him. Logan can still crush him. Greg attaches to the stronger nearby authority because that is how he has survived every room so far.

The choice is cowardly only if one imagines Greg has access to the Roys’ confidence. He does not. His calculations are the calculations of someone who can be dismissed, mocked, or sacrificed without much ceremony. He chooses badly in moral terms, but accurately in institutional terms. He swims beside the nearest shark because distance from the shark has never protected him.

After Logan’s death, Greg’s selective hardening is harder to miss. He fires people through a screen. He participates in the “Disgusting Brothers” routine with Tom. He tries to ingratiate himself with Matsson’s circle. He is more openly transactional, but the awkward casing stays intact. His ambition still outruns his vocabulary.

That tension gives “America Decides” its force for him. Greg learns that Shiv is working with Matsson and takes the information to Kendall. The betrayal is clean enough to matter. It exposes Shiv at a crucial moment and changes what Kendall thinks he knows about the night, the deal, and his sister’s part in both. Greg does not deliver the information with confidence or vision. He does not become the author of the outcome. He copies the behavior he has watched for years: identify the vulnerability, carry it to the person who can use it, and become briefly indispensable.

It is imitation, not mastery. Greg can now betray someone in a way that alters the room, but he still lacks the fluency of those who have treated rooms as inheritance since childhood. He has learned timing in fragments. He has learned that a secret gains value when delivered to the hungriest listener. He has learned that looking harmless can survive the act of harm. He has not learned how to appear secure.

## The Sticker and the Best Available Fate

The finale returns Greg’s education to Tom in its bluntest form. Greg overhears enough of Matsson’s plan to know that Shiv will not be made CEO and that Tom is the likely choice. Once again, he has information before he has a settled allegiance. Once again, he spends it by warning the person he thinks can still act on it. This time the move is riskier because it touches Tom directly. Greg is no longer hiding paper in the aftermath of an order. He is interfering with the final arrangement before it hardens.

Tom answers with his hands. The slap fight strips their relationship down to its oldest materials: fear, dependency, insult, need. It is ridiculous, but it is not a joke in the usual sense. Their violence has always been displaced into management, ceremony, and language. Now it is childish and bare. Tom hits because Greg has tried to trade the most valuable knowledge in the room. Greg hits back because he has learned that submission alone does not guarantee protection.

The fight is a fitting graduation and an unsettling one. Greg proves that Tom has taught him too well. Tom proves that the lesson never promised equality. They are both outsiders to the Roy bloodline, but the finale does not make that shared condition noble. It makes it transactional. Tom is chosen because Matsson needs an American operator who can absorb direction and look official while doing so. Greg remains valuable because he knows too much, asks for too little, and can still be kept close.

The sticker completes the relationship more precisely than reconciliation would. Tom places it on Greg after winning the position. The gesture is proprietary, childish, and almost tender, but it is not generous. He does not raise Greg into partnership. He annexes him. Greg is part of Tom’s inventory, a familiar subordinate from the climb, a witness with uses, too compromised to discard casually.

The final mark sums up Greg’s progression. He has survived, and survival in this world is a real achievement. He has moved from the mascot suit to executive adjacency, from vomiting through the company’s eyes to standing near the man selected to run it. But Greg’s success is attachment. He ends near the winner, obviously not as the winner.

The arrangement already contains the cost. Greg has converted humiliation into access, hesitation into cover, and conscience into a delay mechanism. He has learned from every insult without becoming secure, and from every opportunity without becoming admirable.

His luck lies in remaining attachable. He can be claimed after betrayal because he is useful enough to keep and diminished enough not to threaten the person keeping him. He survives by orbiting rather than ruling, by staying close to authority without being mistaken for it. The final sticker is less a reward than a label. Greg belongs to the victory, but only as something the victor has decided to retain.
