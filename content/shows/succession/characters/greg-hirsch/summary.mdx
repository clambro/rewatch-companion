Greg Hirsch is defined by a survival pattern in which humiliation becomes leverage and proximity becomes his only real credential: he enters the series literally inside Waystar’s costume, sickened by the brand yet already implicated in it, and the show treats his awkwardness not as innocence but as a usable form of disposability. He learns to endure misrecognition, mockery, and threatened exclusion by pausing, asking, and complying just enough to stay near power, which makes him a kind of accidental recording device who can hear what others say around him. Tom Wambsgans turns this into a curriculum, making cruelty into instruction and teaching Greg that intimacy in this world is built through alternating favor and threat; Greg’s growth is not fluency or principled resistance but the selective hardening that lets him keep something back, delay commitment, and convert shame into small acts of leverage. The cruises papers, later betrayals, and his instinct to bring information to the nearest hungry authority show that Greg’s morality is mostly procedural hesitation, not refusal, and his choices are consistently cowardly in ethical terms but accurate in institutional terms. By the end, he has learned to exploit what he has seen without ever appearing secure, becoming useful precisely because he asks for too little and can still be attached after betrayal. The final sticker from Tom seals the central irony: Greg has survived and advanced, but only as an annexed subordinate whose captivity is the cost of remaining close enough to matter.
