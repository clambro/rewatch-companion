---
title: "Connor Roy"
dek: "The eldest Roy turns neglect into ceremonies of importance, finding not freedom but a bargain that can hold."
---

Connor Roy begins _Succession_ with an advantage the show immediately makes unusable. He is Logan’s eldest son, but the dynasty has already learned to proceed without him. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman orbit Waystar Royco because Logan has made the company the only place where a child can matter to him. Connor stands outside that succession contest and insists the distance is chosen. The insistence matters. Without it, his life would look too plainly like exclusion.

He is not outside the family’s money, habits, or theatrical reflexes. His removal from the corporate plot depends on the same inheritance that keeps the others trapped near Logan. The ranch at Austerlitz gives his exclusion a landscape: land, staff, expensive food, historical decor, and the posture of a man who believes distance from Manhattan has produced independence. Yet Austerlitz never feels like an exit from Roy life. It is Roy money arranged into a frontier tableau.

Connor can play host, landowner, amateur statesman, custodian of family memory. He can invite everyone to a therapeutic weekend and present himself as the sibling with the broadest view. The more he occupies that role, the more visible the staging becomes. His language about authenticity often sounds borrowed from books, television, campaign fantasy, and inherited ideas of ordinary life. He wants reality, but he reaches for it through props.

The family understands this before he does. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman mock him because mockery is their common idiom, but Logan’s treatment is more revealing. Logan rarely needs to defeat Connor. He treats him as already outside the contest. That indifference is one of the show’s quieter humiliations, because the younger three at least receive Logan’s attention as pressure. Kendall gets groomed and discarded. Shiv gets summoned and patronized. Roman gets tested, teased, and intermittently invited closer. Connor receives neglect so settled that it barely announces itself.

This position gives Connor moments of real perception. In “Rehearsal,” when he calls his siblings “needy love sponges” and says he is “the one without love,” he identifies the mechanism that keeps all of them returning to Logan. The line is funny because Connor delivers even his hurt with a performer’s timing. It also lands because he knows more than his siblings usually credit him for knowing. His self-mockery is not empty. It contains an account of his place in the family.

But Connor has not learned to live without love. He has learned to request it indirectly, through titles, ceremonies, polling numbers, seating charts, and public ratification of private arrangements. He wants less than his siblings in one sense. He does not need to become CEO. But he still needs a room, a role, and an audience willing to agree that his version of importance counts.

## Inheritance Without a Job

The presidential campaign is Connor’s clearest attempt to build a public life outside Logan’s company, and the joke has a precise shape: he can only do it with Logan’s money and the Roy name. The campaign is not a disposable subplot about an eccentric rich man. It is Connor’s most elaborate effort to convert inheritance into stature. He wants politics to do for him what Waystar does for Kendall, Shiv, and Roman, to provide a structure in which attention looks earned.

Season 2 makes the pattern legible. Connor speaks about taxes, liberty, and principle with the solemnity of someone delivering doctrine rather than testing an argument. He wants the gravity of conviction without the discipline that would make conviction consequential. Staff, messaging, donor logistics, and polling become accessories around a self-image. He can hire the apparatus of seriousness, but he cannot submit to its ordinary demands.

His anti-tax politics sharpen the absurdity because they depend on the fortune that has insulated him from the obligations he resents. The show does not need to refute his platform at length. It simply places the rhetoric beside the life. Connor’s supposed independence arrives through inherited capital, family access, and campaign infrastructure he treats as proof that he has become a statesman. He wants to occupy importance the way someone might occupy a decorated room, by standing in it long enough and hoping the walls confer authority.

The campaign also shows how marginality can become useful. Connor’s vote share is ridiculous until it becomes negotiable. His small percentage can matter in an election. His withdrawal can be courted. His vanity can be priced. He imagines himself as a national actor; other people treat him as a bargaining chip with an unusually expensive emotional life.

His proposal to Willa belongs to the same pattern. It comes while the campaign gives him a new fantasy of public consequence. Marriage becomes part of the architecture. A candidate needs a wife, a life needs a shape, and Connor needs Willa’s presence to be legible as something more stable than an arrangement. The proposal is vanity, fear, and sincere desire at once.

Willa’s hesitation refuses the conversion Connor wants. He can ask the question, present the ring, and imagine a future in which she completes the picture. He cannot make her answer into romance by force of staging. By the time the wedding arrives, the show has already clarified Connor’s method. He selects a role, funds the setting, and hopes reality will adjust.

## The Rehearsal as Exposure

Willa is the relationship that most carefully resists both sentimentality and contempt. The terms between her and Connor are never hidden. He pays for a life in which she can write, live comfortably, and remain near him. She offers companionship, patience, tact, and eventually a commitment that is neither pure transaction nor unambiguous romance. The show’s discipline lies in refusing to simplify this. Willa is not merely using him. Connor is not merely a fool. Their bond has affection in it, but affection does not erase the bargain.

That ambiguity is exactly what Connor keeps trying to settle through form. A proposal, a rehearsal dinner, a wedding, a campaign, a possible ambassadorship: each supplies a structure that can make a messy private arrangement appear official. He wants the label to stabilize the feeling. Willa, more lucid about the costs, knows that public form can also become a trap.

In “Rehearsal,” her exit from the dinner punctures the machinery. She says she cannot do this, and Connor responds by trying to flee the world he has built for the occasion. He wants to go to a “real bar,” somewhere with men who work with their hands, a place that will release him from the polished artificiality of his own event. The idea is funny because Connor cannot stop curating even his escape. At the bar, he orders a Belgian weissbier but specifies not a Hoegaarden. He wants roughness with preferences.

The scene keeps Connor inside the Roy method even when he claims to be leaving it. His chase after ordinary life is another production. The bar becomes a new set: abandoned groom, man of the people, wounded regular guy, connoisseur of unvarnished experience. His siblings come with him, bringing money, impatience, and the family habit of turning every feeling into a bit. Willa remains absent but central. Even the attempted escape circles the ceremony.

Yet the episode also makes Connor briefly more legible than the running joke usually allows. His collapse after Willa leaves is not dignified, but it has the rough outline of knowledge. He understands that the marriage may be a bargain. He understands that his siblings do not usually look at him closely. He understands that his place in the family has trained him to translate deprivation into performance before anyone else can name it.

The siblings’ response is unusual because Connor is usually too unthreatening to merit their full attention. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman can afford to dismiss him because he does not compete for the thing they believe matters most. In “Rehearsal,” his distress briefly interrupts that hierarchy. They do not become tender people, and their care remains awkward, partial, and self-involved, but they do follow him.

His karaoke performance of Leonard Cohen’s “Famous Blue Raincoat” then alters the register without escaping performance. Connor often performs to bluff his way into dignity. Here the performance lets something less guarded pass through. The song’s address to estrangement and old injury catches a frequency he rarely manages in conversation. He is still staging himself, but the staging permits a kind of truth.

His declaration that he is “the one without love” works the same way. It is theatrical, self-pitying, and accurate enough to disturb the room. Connor hears the joke in himself before the others can make it. He turns his exclusion into a line because a line can be controlled. But the control does not cancel the knowledge. In that moment, his self-mockery becomes evidence rather than disguise.

## A Wedding Overtaken

“Connor’s Wedding” gives him the public ceremony he has wanted and removes the audience from him. The episode begins with his attention on details, the managerial anxieties of a groom who needs the room to hold together. Guests arrive, instructions circulate, and Connor tries to keep the event legible as his. Logan’s death occurs elsewhere, in transit, over phones, through fragments of information passed among siblings. The title names Connor’s wedding, but the family’s attention is pulled toward Logan’s absence.

The cruelty is exact. Connor has built a day meant to make him visible, and even on that day Logan commands the room by leaving it forever. Connor asks whether Logan ever liked him, then finds a bleak comfort in the thought that if Logan never liked him, at least nothing has been newly taken away. He has lowered his expectations so far that grief becomes another exercise in limiting disappointment.

The wedding still happens. That matters. It does not become the triumphant confirmation Connor imagined, but it becomes a real choice within constrained terms. Willa stays. Connor accepts a diminished version of the day. The ceremony’s loss of grandeur may even make the marriage more honest than his original fantasy allowed. He does not get the full pageant. He gets a wife who knows the arrangement and enters it anyway.

That outcome also keeps him from becoming a pure object of pity. Connor’s hurt is real, but he is not innocent of the bargain. He has used money to secure proximity, and Willa has accepted the terms with increasing clarity. The wedding does not erase that history. It formalizes it under worse conditions than Connor wanted and better conditions than he feared. In a series where people often mistake acquisition for intimacy, Connor’s marriage is unusually frank about what has been purchased and what has not.

## Compromise as an Ending

Connor’s ending avoids the final succession struggle without freeing him from the habits that formed it. In the finale, Kendall, Shiv, and Roman move toward the board vote as if the company can still settle who they are to Logan and to one another. Connor remains outside that contest. He has never had a plausible path to Waystar’s throne, and Logan’s death does not create one.

But his distance from the vote is not independence. He remains inside Roy family economics and Roy family performance. The possible ambassadorship is a perfectly Connor-shaped role: public, ceremonial, obtained through bargaining rather than mandate. It would let him represent something without requiring the political legitimacy his campaign never achieved. Even this fantasy of service arrives as costume and arrangement.

The scenes around Logan’s apartment show the scale of Connor’s inheritance. He moves through the remnants of his father’s life with the air of someone trying to inherit atmosphere as much as property. Objects, rooms, and domestic plans become ways of placing himself in a lineage that never fully admitted him. He is still turning inheritance into a role: custodian, husband, tasteful son, man with a household. The reflex remains, but it has found a smaller arena than Kendall’s claim, Shiv’s calculation, or Roman’s recoil.

His marriage to Willa is the same kind of settlement. It neither redeems nor condemns him. She has made a practical choice; so has he. Their arrangement includes dependence, theater, convenience, and a thin but real form of mutual accommodation. Willa knows what Connor can give her. Connor knows, more than he admits, what she cannot give him. The compromise is not romantic in the way he once wanted, but it is livable.

The ranch did not make him independent. The campaign did not make him serious. The wedding did not make Logan look at him. His marriage does not solve the absence he keeps naming through jokes and ceremonies. Yet by the end, he has assembled a life that can continue because it asks less of reality than his siblings’ ambitions do.

Connor makes one of the show’s larger logics visible from the side. Inheritance without aspiration becomes a style of self-invention. Self-invention without power becomes another costume. He ends where he is most himself, peripheral to the main contest, protected by money, faintly absurd, and sustained by an agreement that contains just enough recognition to hold.
