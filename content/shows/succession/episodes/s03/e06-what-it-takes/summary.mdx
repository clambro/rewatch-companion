“What It Takes” treats the Future Freedom Summit as a series of auditions in which the Roys read presidential politics through the same status logic they use at Waystar: candidates are evaluated less for policy than for whether they can dominate a room, absorb pressure, and make the family’s instincts feel national. Boyer’s institutional polish reads as managerial weakness, Salgado’s bargain tempts Shiv with the fantasy of outside certification, and Mencken wins because he turns menace into composure, the exact social style Roman and Logan admire when they mistake hardening for seriousness. The episode’s cramped spaces, from hotel corridors to the family suite, keep everyone in transit and underline that selection is always provisional, while Caroline’s marriage news and Tuscany invitation recur as another public reordering of private family life by people already outside the room. Kendall’s DOJ material functions as a failed counterweight because Lisa Arthur refuses his self-narrating urgency, and Tom and Greg’s diner conversation turns prison and blame into the series’ usual calculus of transferred exposure. The ending photo-op makes Logan’s choice literal: Mencken is not just selected but framed with the Roys, and Shiv’s minimal distance cannot prevent incorporation, showing how the family’s longing for an external validator ends by putting their own domination instincts into public, portable form.
