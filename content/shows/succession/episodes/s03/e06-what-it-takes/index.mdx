## Episode Summary

"What It Takes," season 3, episode 6 of _Succession_, sends Logan and the Waystar circle to the Future Freedom Summit in Virginia, where the Roys assess Vice President Boyer, Congressman Rick Salgado, and Congressman Jeryd Mencken as possible successors to the departing president. Shiv tries to turn Salgado’s interest into a path toward Waystar, Roman is drawn toward Mencken, Connor pushes his own candidacy, Kendall’s Department of Justice cooperation falters under Lisa Arthur’s scrutiny, Tom and Greg discuss prison and blame at a diner, and Roman learns that Caroline is marrying Peter Munion and inviting the family to Tuscany. The episode ends with Logan choosing Mencken and pulling the family into a photo-op that Shiv reluctantly joins.

## Waiting to Be Selected

The Future Freedom Summit is built out of rooms where people wait to be sorted. Candidates wait to be examined, donors wait for signs, aides wait for instructions, and the Roys move through the hotel as if the presidency were a senior appointment inside a company they happen to dominate. The episode’s spaces matter. Corridors, suites, lobbies, ballrooms, bathrooms, and a diner keep the characters in transit, never quite at the place where a decision can feel settled. Everyone speaks as if the next move will clarify the order of things, but the staging keeps that clarity provisional.

The summit does not give the Roys a civic vocabulary. It gives them another social room to read. The candidate dinner is the episode’s central audition, and the question is not which man has the strongest platform. The question is who can make an anxious, status-conscious table accept his terms. Boyer enters with the obvious résumé. He is the sitting vice president, the continuity figure, the person whose institutional position should make him difficult to dismiss. In the room, those credentials make him look managerial and exposed. He appears to need ordinary processes to hold him upright. Logan reads that as weakness.

Salgado understands the occasion more sharply. He offers Shiv a clean bargain: help him, and he will make her advancement at Waystar part of his political project. The offer has a pleasing directness. It flatters Shiv’s sense that she can translate between public life and family business better than her brothers can. It also gives her a way to imagine that Logan’s delays can be bypassed by an outside guarantee. If Salgado owes her, then perhaps her future at Waystar can be certified from beyond Logan’s reach.

The episode keeps exposing the flaw in that logic. Shiv can bargain only inside the room Logan controls. She can bring Salgado’s offer to him, interpret it, argue for it, and clothe it in political caution, but she cannot make it bind him. Her poise in these scenes is part of the pressure. Sarah Snook plays Shiv as someone refusing to let irritation disturb the surface. She listens, calculates, smiles when a smile is useful, and keeps turning the conversation toward a future in which she remains convertible: political consultant, corporate heir, internal adult. The problem is that every possible version still requires Logan to count it.

Mencken enters differently. He does not present himself as a manageable asset. He carries threat as social style. His scenes with Roman are effective because they do not reduce him to shouting or doctrine. He lets danger arrive in tempo, in the faintly amused way he receives provocation, in the sense that accusation will not embarrass him. Roman needles him because Roman needs to test the surface. He asks the crude question, makes the obscene turn, prods at the word everyone else is trying to route around. Mencken absorbs the pressure and gives it back as composure.

That is what Roman recognizes. He has no deep policy interest here. He responds to a man who can take discomfort in the room and make it look like evidence of his own seriousness. Roman often uses insult to puncture official speech, but the tactic usually leaves him in nervous motion, looking for the next laugh, the next recoil, the next proof that he has not been caught wanting something. Mencken seems to have what Roman imitates: the ability to make provocation steady rather than jittery.

Logan’s choice follows from that social reading. He does not conduct policy analysis. He identifies the candidate who can harden when challenged. Boyer looks like a caretaker. Salgado looks like a dealmaker. Mencken looks like someone who can force a room to organize around him. That distinction matters because the Roys do not encounter politics as citizens or ideologues. They encounter it as a search for an external figure who might certify their own instincts back to them. Mencken offers a political version of Logan’s preferred atmosphere: objection becomes fuel, fear becomes a credential, explanation comes later.

## Family News in a Public Corridor

The episode’s sharpest cutaway arrives almost casually. Roman learns from someone outside the family that Caroline is marrying Peter Munion and that the children have been invited to Tuscany. The information should belong to the intimate category of family news, but it reaches Roman as social gossip inside a political hotel. That is why it lands with such strange force. The Roy household rearranges itself in public while the siblings are trying to sort out national politics.

Roman’s reaction is not clean hurt. It is disturbance routed through status. Who knew? Why did someone else know first? What does it mean to be informed through a stranger? The scene makes the family’s private life obey the same rules as the summit. Information moves before feeling catches up. By the time the children can respond, an arrangement has already been made elsewhere.

When Roman and Shiv call Kendall, the news briefly gathers the siblings into a shared register. They are not negotiating a board vote or parsing a press line. They are absorbing the fact that their mother has made a new family formation and notified them from a distance. Even then, the moment cannot become simple. The Roys have been trained to treat every disclosure as a ranking. Inclusion and exclusion matter as much as the marriage itself. Caroline’s invitation to Tuscany will later become the setting in which their alliance against Logan forms and fails, but here it first appears as an abrupt displacement: their parents keep reorganizing the family without them, then expect them to appear correctly in the new arrangement.

Connor’s campaign sits nearby in a more comic but related key. The summit’s atmosphere lets him hear possibility in every ceremonial phrase. If the next president might be in the room, why not him? His candidacy depends on the hope that setting can produce seriousness. A conference, a donor class, a presidential field, a family discussion: these are the props through which he tries to enter the category under debate.

The family suite makes the humiliation procedural. Connor is not dramatically crushed. He is entertained, briefly factored in, then passed over. That is a more exact form of dismissal for him. He wants a title to make people stop treating him as optional, but the conversation itself proves his optionality. The room can hold his fantasy as texture, not as a proposal.

The suite scene gathers the family’s separate motives into one cramped selection process. Shiv argues for Salgado partly because she has found a route that might preserve her own future. Roman argues for Mencken because he trusts the feel of menace when it has good manners. Connor offers himself because the occasion resembles recognition. Logan listens and lets each child reveal the personal need tucked inside the political judgment. The hotel suite, set above the summit’s public churn, turns the presidency into a family purchase discussed from a private room.

## Counterweights That Do Not Hold

Kendall’s Department of Justice storyline runs alongside the summit as the episode’s failed counterweight. In theory, it should offer a different standard. While Logan tests candidates by appetite and room presence, Kendall deals with lawyers, documents, testimony, and federal scrutiny. He wants the cruises material to do what his press conference promised: turn moral exposure into a case that can wound Logan cleanly.

Lisa Arthur’s impatience breaks that fantasy. She does not perform Logan’s cruelty, and she does not enter the family’s usual rhythm of insult and withheld affection. Her frustration is professional, which makes it harder for Kendall to turn into fuel. She tells him, in effect, that the material is not arranging itself into the story he needs and that his own performance has made matters worse. Kendall cannot accept a process that will not mirror his urgency. He fires the person telling him that the revolt is weakening.

The crosscutting between Virginia, the legal office, and the diner gives the episode its shape. These institutions look different, but the scene structure keeps rhyming them. In the summit hotel, candidates are sorted. In the legal room, Kendall is evaluated and found wanting. In the diner, Tom and Greg discuss who will absorb punishment. The settings vary in polish, but each scene asks who will be selected, who will be sacrificed, and who can make someone else’s exposure useful.

Kendall fails because the DOJ will not accept self-narration as evidence. He can speak in the language of accountability, but he cannot submit to the discipline that would make accountability more than branding. Jeremy Strong lets the confidence fray in small irritations: the impatience with Lisa, the refusal to hear the limits of the papers, the immediate turn toward replacement when the room does not give him the verdict he expected. Kendall wants an institution to ratify his rebellion without requiring him to become smaller, slower, and more exact.

Tom, by contrast, has begun to make himself small enough to survive. The diner scene with Greg lowers the episode’s volume, but it is one of its most unnerving mirrors. Tom rehearses prison as a future condition rather than a sudden calamity. He orders, sits, talks, and imagines incarceration with a flattened dread that would be less frightening if it became panic. Matthew Macfadyen plays him as almost administratively resigned. Fear has become part of the workday.

Greg asks the question in the only form he knows: can Tom take his portion too? It is a grotesque request, but it is also the logical product of his education. Protection in this world does not arrive as care. It arrives as transferred exposure. Someone takes the papers, someone eats the blame, someone stands in the wrong place when the cameras or investigators arrive. Tom agrees to carry Greg’s risk because he has already accepted himself as the likeliest container for other people’s costs. The gesture has tenderness in it, but the tenderness comes attached to hierarchy.

When Kendall later meets Tom in the same diner, the room has already been marked as a place where fear becomes a bargain. Kendall offers him safety if he turns against Logan. Tom refuses with a cold reading of history. He has watched Kendall challenge his father and fail, while Logan survives crisis after crisis. This is not loyalty in any noble sense. It is Tom choosing the side that looks more durable under pressure.

Kendall’s attempt to photograph Tom as he leaves is a thin substitute for commitment. He tries to turn mere proximity into a usable fact, as if an image of a meeting could replace the testimony he failed to secure. The gesture is especially weak in an episode that will end with Logan doing a stronger version of the same thing. Kendall makes an image after persuasion fails. Logan will make an image that completes the choice.

## Standing in the Frame

The final photo-op is not decoration after the decision. It is the decision taking public form. Logan’s alignment with Mencken becomes real when bodies are arranged, faces are turned outward, and cameras are allowed to fix the relation. The episode has been full of temporary rooms and passing conversations, but the ending tries to stop motion long enough to create a record.

Logan’s handling of Shiv is the essential action. Her objections to Mencken have already been overruled, but the photograph asks for something more compromising than silence. It asks her to appear. Logan does not answer her argument. He reduces the matter to belonging: is she part of the family or not? That conversion is one of his most effective habits. He takes a disagreement that has content and turns it into a test of attachment.

Shiv’s compromise is exact and insufficient. She agrees to stand at the opposite end from Mencken, preserving a visible sliver of distance. It lets her maintain, for herself, that she has not fully consented. The photograph will not preserve the caveat. It will show the Roy family lined up with the candidate Logan chose. Her posture carries reluctance, but the image records incorporation.

Mencken’s smile inside that arrangement clarifies his function in the episode. He has not won an argument so much as survived the social audition better than the others. He can let the Roys mistake his theatrical force for seriousness. Later, when ATN helps turn his contested election-night position into broadcast reality, the danger of that mistake becomes more explicit. "What It Takes" is quieter and more preliminary. It shows the attraction before the consequences have hardened, at the moment when a family trained to admire domination recognizes a public ally who can give their private reflexes a national face.

The episode matters because it extends the series’ long interest in recognition beyond the company without changing the family’s habits. The Roys look outward for a figure who can certify them, but the outward figure they choose only reflects them back in harsher public form. Logan pulls them into line, Shiv tries to leave a gap inside the arrangement, Roman sees the thrill of the man he helped elevate, and the camera makes the alignment portable. The cost is not only that they stand near Mencken. It is that, once inside his frame, their reluctance becomes part of the picture too.
