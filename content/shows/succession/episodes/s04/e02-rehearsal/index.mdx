## Episode Summary

"Rehearsal" follows Kendall, Shiv, and Roman as they consider joining Sandi and Stewy’s attempt to pressure Matsson for more money on the GoJo sale. Connor’s disrupted wedding rehearsal leads the siblings to a karaoke bar, where Logan confronts them and fails to stop the maneuver. At ATN, Tom and Greg handle Kerry’s failed anchor audition. The episode ends with Logan privately drawing Roman back in, asking him to help with Matsson and tempting him with ATN.

## The Alliance as Improvisation

The GoJo dispute gives the siblings a plausible business reason to stand together. Sandi and Stewy think the board can push Matsson for a higher price. Shiv is primed for aggression after learning that Tom has followed Logan’s divorce advice and consulted major attorneys. Kendall is restless after the Pierce bid, eager to convert doubt into forward motion. Roman starts from a more practical place, warning that the move could endanger the sale and their payout. His resistance is not maturity in any settled sense, but it is accurate. He recognizes the Roy habit of disguising retaliation as strategy.

Kendall’s private call with Matsson changes the argument without appearing to. Matsson tells him that if the board presses too hard, he may walk. Kendall withholds that information from Shiv and Roman, then continues moving toward the Sandi-Stewy play. The concealment contaminates the siblings’ collective posture. Kendall can still speak the language of shareholder value, but the viewer knows he is acting on a second track. He has been told that the maneuver could kill the deal, and that possibility appeals to the part of him still looking for a clean way to wound Logan.

That hidden call makes Roman’s later defection less isolated than it first appears. The sibling bloc is compromised before Logan touches it. Roman has been texting Logan on his birthday. Kendall is withholding Matsson’s warning. Shiv is using the deal as an outlet for anger at Tom and, through Tom, at Logan’s continued reach into her private life. The alliance looks strongest when the three of them speak as a unit, but small private transactions keep moving inside that unit. Their independence has no common interior. It is a temporary convergence of separate injuries.

The counterplay is neither simple competence nor simple spite. The siblings are not wrong that Matsson may be underpaying. They are also not free from the old reflex: make Logan react, then treat his reaction as proof that they exist outside him. The show keeps the business case alive while showing how much excess feeling has attached itself to it. A cleaner boardroom drama would ask whether the children are right about price. "Rehearsal" asks whether they can act together when each of them is secretly negotiating a different relationship to Logan’s attention.

## The Karaoke Room

Connor’s rehearsal dinner begins as the wrong room for everyone. Willa is absent. Connor is monitoring her location and trying to preserve a social event already hollowed out by embarrassment. The siblings come along, but their presence is partial. Their minds remain with GoJo, Matsson, Stewy, and Logan. Connor’s wedding ritual becomes a waiting room for their corporate fight.

The karaoke bar sharpens that displacement. It is anonymous, awkward, and almost aggressively undignified, a place where the Roys cannot rely on the usual furniture of rank. Connor wants the evening to become recognizably festive. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman cannot give him that. They can attend the event, order drinks, and tolerate the room, but they keep converting the occasion into another branch of the Waystar dispute.

Connor’s choice of Leonard Cohen’s "Famous Blue Raincoat" is funny because it is so self-conscious, but the show does not let the joke exhaust the moment. The song gives Connor a borrowed style of wounded elegance. He uses taste as a brace against humiliation, selecting something melancholy and literate enough to protect him from the rawness of being abandoned at his own pre-wedding event. The performance is neither triumphant nor pathetic in a simple way. It is Connor using ceremony, music, and cultivated sadness to keep himself assembled.

Logan’s arrival changes the room immediately. He enters with Kerry, who remains there as a reminder of another failed audition for legitimacy, and the room reorders itself around him. Kendall and Shiv push accusations toward him. Roman softens and deflects. Connor tries to keep the evening from disappearing entirely into their fight. Logan does not need to dominate the room by volume. He lets the children spend themselves, then answers with the sentence that defines the encounter: "I love you, but you are not serious people."

The line lands because it is both tactical and perceptive. Logan has come to stop them from interfering with the Matsson sale. His apology is instrumental and incomplete. He cannot name what he is sorry for because naming it would make the scene morally specific, and moral specificity is not what he wants. He wants compliance. Yet the practical weakness of his position does not make his diagnosis false. In the karaoke room, Kendall and Shiv are full of argument, Roman of recoil, Connor of displaced grievance. Logan cannot persuade them as a bloc, but he can see where the bloc will split.

Kendall and Shiv try to force the scene into an accounting of injury. They point toward childhood damage, neglect, and violence. Roman refuses the frame even when the facts concern him, deflecting the reference to Logan hitting him by making himself the joke. That deflection is not forgiveness. It is Roman protecting the old arrangement by refusing to let anyone else narrate his place in it. He can mock Logan, fear him, read him, and resent him, but he still resists any version of the story that would make him plainly harmed and therefore separate.

Connor is the sharpest observer because he has the least investment in winning the immediate argument. His monologue after Logan leaves is not a redemptive speech. It is an ugly, lucid self-description. He tells the others that the advantage of having a family that does not love you is that you learn to live without it. He calls them "needy love sponges" and describes himself as a plant growing on rocks, feeding on insects that die inside him. The image is grotesque because Connor’s clarity has not made him healthy. It has made him durable.

What Connor sees is the transaction beneath the siblings’ defiance. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman are still chasing Logan through opposition. They want to beat him in a way that makes him acknowledge the loss as theirs. Connor’s deprivation has taken a different shape. He wants ceremonies: a wedding, a campaign, the title and optics of importance. He is ridiculous because he keeps mistaking forms for substance, but he is not confused about Logan’s emotional economy. He knows that attention can look like love when a child has been trained to accept the substitute.

Connor goes home and finds Willa in bed. The scene does not purify the relationship. It lets the arrangement continue. Willa’s return gives Connor enough to proceed, not everything he wants. That is consistent with his arc across the series: he survives by accepting compromised forms that Kendall, Shiv, and Roman would experience as intolerable diminishment. In this episode, that limited acceptance makes him the one sibling able to describe the others accurately.

## Kerry and the Administrative Version

The Kerry subplot compresses the episode’s logic into office procedure. Kerry has Logan’s proximity and assumes it can be converted into ATN authority. Her audition tape proves otherwise. The newsroom can be frightened by Logan, but the camera still tests timing, face, voice, and credibility. Favor can force access to the audition. It cannot make the tape persuasive.

Tom understands the danger of saying this plainly. Kerry’s ambition is attached to Logan’s desire, so a professional verdict becomes a personal risk. Tom therefore turns the problem into a chain of delegated discomfort. He frames, softens, evades, and finally passes the task to Greg. Greg, with his usual mixture of cowardice and clumsy survival instinct, delivers the message badly enough to make the cruelty visible.

The subplot is small and precise: an administrative parallel to the sibling plot. No one wants to own the humiliating truth, so the truth moves downward until it reaches the person with the least protection. Tom has learned how to survive by translating embarrassment into procedure. Greg has learned that being used still leaves him in the room. Their handling of Kerry shows the same logic without the vocabulary of inheritance: access is tested, found wanting, and then managed through someone more disposable.

Logan’s ATN speech sits beside this with sharper menace. He wants the network "faster, lighter, meaner, wilder," which is less a policy program than a desired nervous system. He imagines ATN as the part of the empire that can still respond directly to his appetite after the GoJo sale. Kerry cannot embody that speed. Tom and Greg can administer the rejection. The newsroom can absorb the pressure. Logan is rehearsing a reduced but concentrated version of himself, one routed through the people and instruments still responsive to him.

## Roman as the Child Still Available

The ending gives the episode its consequence. Logan loses the karaoke-room negotiation in the immediate sense. Kendall and Shiv do not fold. Roman does not openly abandon them there. The GoJo counterplay continues. But Logan does not need to defeat the siblings together if he can separate one of them afterward.

Roman is the obvious target because his attachment to Logan remains the most physically legible. His jokes do not conceal his need so much as keep it moving. He can diagnose the manipulation while still wanting the invitation. Earlier in the episode, his birthday text to Logan exposes this availability in miniature. It is a small gesture, easy to mock, but it shows that Roman has preserved a private line to the person he is supposedly resisting with his siblings.

Logan’s final offer is calibrated to that weakness. He does not give Roman an apology of substance. He gives him a job-shaped form of intimacy. Roman can help with Matsson. Roman can be useful. Roman can have ATN, or at least the suggestion of it, if he comes back into Logan’s orbit. The offer works because it joins practical responsibility to paternal selection. Roman is not asked to betray his siblings in a grand declaration. He is invited to step into a role that feels adult because it comes from Logan’s mouth.

Matsson is crucial here. Roman has already shown an ability to handle him through informality, obscenity, and a kind of damaged candor that the more polished Waystar figures cannot easily imitate. Logan recognizes a usable skill. That recognition is more dangerous than flattery alone. Roman is most susceptible when the praise has evidence behind it. He wants to believe that Logan sees something real in him, not merely a weakness to exploit. The episode allows both readings to stand. Roman does have a skill with Matsson. Logan also knows exactly how to turn that skill into a leash.

The completed series makes the scene sharper without requiring the finale to carry it. Roman’s later attempt to become hard after Logan’s death, especially around ATN and Mencken, depends on the same borrowed authorization visible here. He can perform severity when he believes he is extending Logan’s will or occupying Logan’s chair. He cannot sustain it when the performance requires an internal center. The funeral exposes that limit brutally, but "Rehearsal" has already shown the mechanism in quieter form: Roman accepts delegated purpose as recognition.

The last beat belongs to Roman because Logan’s method still works most cleanly on him. Kendall supplies the concealed appetite that keeps the GoJo maneuver unstable. Shiv’s anger helps keep the sibling bloc in motion. Tom and Greg show the office version of the same downward logic. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman can still sit in rooms together, make plans, and sound aligned. What they cannot do is protect the alliance from the private routes Logan has built into each of them.
