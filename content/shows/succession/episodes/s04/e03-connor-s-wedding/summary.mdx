“Connor’s Wedding” treats Logan’s death as a broken transmission rather than a cathartic spectacle, stripping away the usual authority of his presence and forcing the Roys to receive him through damaged audio, procedural language, and inaccessible space. The yacht and plane split makes his end feel like failed delivery: the children are trapped in grief before they can understand it, while Tom, Frank, Karolina, and the other adults immediately convert catastrophe into logistics, disclosure, and market control. Kendall reaches for command structure, Roman is destabilized by the humiliating failure of Logan’s order to fire Gerri, and Shiv is pulled from private avoidance into the public language of corporate continuity. The episode’s title is pointedly miscentered, because Connor’s wedding becomes the one part of life that can still proceed on its own terms; his smaller claim, shaped by exclusion rather than succession fantasy, gives him a clearer grasp of limited reality than his siblings. The result is a first post-Logan test in which grief and procedure are inseparable, and Logan’s absence is defined less by a final message than by the inability of anyone around him to turn loss into anything but work.
