“Church and State” treats Logan’s funeral as a final succession audition in which grief becomes a public test of authority, and the episode’s key insight is that the children can only inherit his habits, not his coherence. The church’s ritual order is braided with the state’s disorder through Mencken, ATN, and the protests outside, so burial becomes a contested political event rather than closure. The eulogies define the episode’s argument: Ewan punctures the Logan myth by naming the childhood damage beneath his brutality, Roman collapses because he cannot sustain performance before the body itself, Kendall succeeds by speaking in Logan’s register and turning paternal force into public command, and Shiv offers the most precise emotional reading even as she remains trapped in the family’s bargain structure. Connor’s sidelined ceremonial ambition underscores that procedure without power cannot secure inheritance. The mausoleum, reception, and street unrest extend the same logic, showing Logan’s legacy as a system of arrangement that still scripts the survivors, while the finale in the protest exposes the limits of inherited posture once the stage of ritual falls away.
