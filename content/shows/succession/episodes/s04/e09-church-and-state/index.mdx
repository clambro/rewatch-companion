## Episode Summary

“Church and State” centers on Logan Roy’s funeral at a New York church the day after ATN has called the presidential election for Jeryd Mencken. Roman is scheduled to deliver the main family eulogy, after practicing it as a display of command, but he breaks down at the lectern in front of Logan’s casket. Ewan unexpectedly speaks first, offering a severe account of Logan’s childhood, guilt, and moral damage. Kendall then takes over with an improvised speech that restores force to the room, and Shiv follows with a more intimate and exact account of Logan’s coldness. Connor’s desired funeral role remains sidelined and contested, his overworked eulogy and ceremonial plans unable to secure him the place he wants. Tom’s expected prominence as a casket-bearer is displaced by his ATN obligations, though his absence and later arrival keep the election inside the funeral’s private drama. After the service, the Roys move through the burial, the mausoleum, and the reception while negotiating Mencken, Matsson, GoJo, and one another. The episode ends with Roman leaving the controlled spaces of mourning and entering the protest outside, where he is swallowed by the street chaos after the funeral.

## The Funeral as a Public Test

“Church and State” makes Logan’s funeral the last room he controls without being in it. Burial is not release. It is the next succession procedure, with candles, stone, flowers, front pews, security, handlers, rivals, former wives, mistresses, executives, politicians, and children all arranged around the vacancy he has left. Logan’s body sits in the church as an object everyone must interpret. The question is no longer whether he will return, punish, bless, or reverse course. The question is who can make his absence behave like authority.

The title names its braid. The church provides ritual, elevation, and inherited language for grief. The state arrives through Mencken’s presence, the disputed election, ATN’s call, the protests outside, and the knowledge that a family decision in a newsroom has become a public fact with bodies in the street. The funeral cannot keep these categories apart. Mencken is not background color at a rich man’s memorial. He is part of the inheritance being contested. So is Matsson, hovering as foreign buyer and tactical partner. So is Tom, kept at ATN by the consequences of the previous night, too useful to be allowed the full dignity of mourning. The church promises order, but the order depends on the same men and institutions that have just produced national disorder.

Logan’s authority always required a room large enough to feel reorganized by him. In life, he did this through interruption, silence, insult, and sudden favors. In death, the arrangement must do it for him. The procession, the stark verticality of the church, the casket’s placement, and the choreography of entrance and seating all convert the man into a structure. His ex-wives and lovers become a row of evidence. Caroline, Marcia, Kerry, and Sally-Anne share a charged proximity that Logan would likely have managed through separation, contempt, or transactional charm. Without him, they form a small counter-ritual, less reconciliation than recognition among women who occupied different stations in the same design.

The mausoleum extends this logic into architecture. Logan’s burial place is absurd, grand, expensive, vulgar, practical, and weirdly consistent with him. It turns inheritance into stone. The children stand before a structure that literalizes the thing they have been circling for four seasons: a father who made shelter and imprisonment hard to distinguish. Connor knows details about it because Connor, more than the others, attends to ceremonial arrangements as if they might grant him standing. Kendall sees the deal. Shiv sees the grotesquerie. Roman can joke only when the casket is not demanding speech from him. The mausoleum is not closure. It is Logan’s last acquisition presented as family destiny.

Formally, the episode keeps cutting between stillness and agitation. The church interior is slow, symmetrical, and ritualized. The crowd outside is mobile, loud, and increasingly dangerous. Inside, speech tries to settle meaning. Outside, meaning has already escaped into consequence. That contrast gives the episode its penultimate force. The series has often staged succession as a private contest that leaks into boardrooms, newsrooms, and markets. Here the leak has become visible. The funeral gathers the elite under sacred cover while the state shudders outside the doors. Logan persists not as a ghost or memory, but as an arrangement that still tells people where to sit, when to stand, whom to flatter, and what kind of force a room will reward.

## The Pulpit and the Children

The eulogies are not sentimental interruptions in the corporate plot. They are the episode’s central trials. Each speaker must answer the same demand: can you speak of Logan in public without being consumed by him? The pulpit turns grief into a competence test. It asks for composure, scale, judgment, timing, and audience control. In other words, it asks for the qualities the children have been trying to inherit.

Ewan’s speech can puncture the Logan myth because it does not need Logan’s approval and does not compete for Logan’s office. That does not make Ewan pure moral wisdom. His righteousness has always coexisted with distance, money, bitterness, and a certain pleasure in condemnation. He stayed near enough to know and far enough to judge. Still, the speech does something no corporate maneuver can do: it names the wound beneath the force without letting the wound excuse the force.

His account of the Atlantic crossing gives Logan a childhood before the legend. The boys sit in silence while the ship’s engines fail. The story is spare, almost anti-dramatic, and that restraint gives it force. Ewan then moves to Rose’s death from polio and Logan’s belief that he had brought the disease home. The detail that no one adequately freed him from that belief matters more than any simple diagnosis of cruelty. It gives shape to Logan’s emotional starvation, to a life in which guilt hardened into command and tenderness narrowed into rationed approval. Ewan can say this because he is not trying to succeed Logan. He can also condemn what Logan made: the meagerness, the fear, the ugliness he fed in others. The speech wounds the room because it refuses the funeral’s expected bargain. It will not let ritual polish the dead man into greatness.

Roman has prepared for a different ceremony. In his apartment, he rehearses the eulogy as a victory lap, turning grief into showmanship. He imagines himself as the son who can “light up” the room, the one who has already chosen a president and now can crown the father by sounding like him. This is Roman’s favored territory: performance, obscenity, contempt, tone. He can borrow brutality as long as it remains mobile. He can joke around death, insult through pain, convert need into a bit.

The casket stops him. At the lectern, Logan is no longer an abstract force he can imitate or a voicemail he can replay. The body is there, hidden and undeniable. Roman’s defenses depend on keeping everything slightly unreal, held in quotation marks, available for mockery or reversal. The church denies him that register. He wants the father’s role, but he cannot endure the father’s stillness. He can perform domination when the room lets him move quickly. He cannot stand before the object that proves the performance has a cost.

Kendall’s success follows directly from Roman’s failure. He steps forward saying he will try to stand in for his brother, but the gesture immediately becomes a public taking-over. He understands what the room now needs: not truth, exactly, and not comfort, but command after exposure. Ewan has opened Logan’s history and Roman has shown the children’s damage. Kendall restores the official language of magnitude. He praises Logan’s appetite, his ability to make things happen, his relation to money as lifeblood and oxygen. The speech works because Kendall does not transcend Logan. He identifies with him.

The scene is both a triumph and a trap. Kendall finally meets the room’s demand for force. He feels the audience respond. Mencken notices. The executives notice. Roman’s weakness creates an opening, and Kendall has enough instinct to occupy it. Yet what he delivers is a polished version of Logan’s discipline, not an escape from it. The rhetoric of life, markets, movement, and appetite lets him translate paternal brutality into public vitality. He does not forgive Logan, defeat him, or outgrow him. He finds a cadence in which becoming Logan sounds like leadership.

The episode sharpens a series-long distinction: the Roy children inherit styles, not power itself. Kendall has spent years training the external signs of command, from investor language to media performance to boardroom aggression. In “Church and State,” the imitation finally lands in a room that wants it. But it lands because the occasion has been built by Logan. Kendall rises inside the father’s script, using Roman’s collapse as proof that someone must speak with force. His speech is a successful imitation of authority under ceremonial pressure. The success is real. So is the captivity.

Shiv’s eulogy changes the temperature. She does not try to match Kendall’s grandeur or Ewan’s indictment. Her speech is more exact about Logan as a father: the bursts of warmth made powerful by deprivation, the coldness, the inability to know what to do with women as whole people. Shiv’s practical intelligence appears here as emotional precision. She can say what the men are circling because she has spent the series translating feeling into usable terms. She knows the pressure of Logan’s attention, and she knows the cost of wanting it.

That clarity does not lift her out of the family’s bargain structure. The speech is the episode’s most human public moment, but Shiv also understands that public humanity has value. She can grieve and position herself in the same breath because she has never been taught a protected form of disclosure. Her later interaction with Tom carries the same ambiguity. The episode allows a brief, weary possibility between them: not romance restored, not trust repaired, but a recognition that their lives remain entangled beyond the immediate war. Tom’s exhaustion, Shiv’s pregnancy, and the aftermath of their balcony fight make the exchange quieter than their usual combat. Still, the show does not turn quiet into absolution. Shiv sees Logan more clearly than Kendall or Roman, and perhaps more clearly than Connor. She remains a Roy, which means insight quickly seeks a deal, a role, a route through someone else’s leverage.

Connor’s place at the funeral is smaller, but the smallness is the point. He has tried to make ceremony create a position for him since the pilot: ranch host, presidential candidate, husband, eldest son, custodian of family pageantry. Here he wants a eulogy, a role, a formal claim on Logan’s public memory. The speech he has prepared is too legalistic, too elaborate, too strange to do what he needs it to do. Willa can admire its formal invention, but the family will not risk it. Connor knows the mausoleum, knows the arrangements, and understands some practical facts about his father that the succession contestants ignore. Yet knowledge of ceremony does not become authority. He can help stage the rite, but he cannot make the rite acknowledge him as central.

## After the Service, the Street

After the funeral, the episode loosens the ritual frame without releasing its pressure. At the burial and reception, the siblings carry their speeches back into negotiation. Kendall’s confidence hardens. Roman’s shame becomes available for management. Shiv continues working Matsson and Mencken, trying to convert her usefulness into a future position. The room has changed, but not enough. Logan is buried, and everyone immediately resumes speaking in the grammar he left behind.

Kendall’s post-service behavior makes the danger of his eulogy clearer. The speech has taught him that force can return if he performs it without apology. With Hugo, with Roman, with the political players, he begins to test a colder voice. His line of thought is recognizably Logan’s: affection does not protect anyone, humiliation can discipline the weak, and contingency justifies cruelty. This is not a new Kendall so much as Kendall discovering that imitation can produce results when the audience is primed for it. The funeral gives him a taste of being believed, which is the most dangerous thing the series can give him before the finale.

Roman has the opposite problem. The church has exposed him, and Kendall knows it. Roman’s humiliation cannot remain private because in this family every wound becomes usable information. His collapse at the lectern strips away the fantasy that he had already mourned Logan in advance. “Pre-grieved” was an administrative solution to an emotional fact. Once the casket forced the fact into view, Roman had no procedure left. Afterward, he can still snarl, joke, provoke, and seek contact through contempt, but the room has seen him fail at the one performance that mattered that day.

The final movement into the street is not an atmospheric add-on. It is the hour’s answer to the church. Inside, Roman could not speak before the casket. Outside, he tries to recover himself by entering the unrest his own choices helped intensify. He moves toward the protesters with the reckless curiosity of someone who wants punishment to arrive as contact. The controlled humiliations of the family no longer suffice. He needs the public world to hit him, or to prove that it cannot.

The riot collapses the title’s two terms into one failing order. The church has finished its rite, but the state’s disorder waits at the exit. ATN’s certainty, Mencken’s claimed victory, and the family’s private calculations have returned as sirens, barricades, bodies, and fear. Roman’s wealth and name do not help him once he steps into the crowd. The son who treated national consequence as a game of dominance becomes a vulnerable body among strangers who do not recognize his private mythology. He taunts, shoves, falls, rejects help, and disappears into movement larger than his ability to control tone.

Logan’s funeral does not close him. It compresses him. The episode gathers his legacy into casket, pulpit, mausoleum, television call, political promise, and street violence. By the end, the surviving Roys have not inherited his authority. They have inherited his habits under conditions where his presence can no longer make those habits cohere. The church gives them one last stage on which to sound like successors. The street shows what remains when the stage door opens.
