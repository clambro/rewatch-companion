## Episode Summary

In _Succession_ Season 4, Episode 5, “Kill List,” Kendall and Roman, newly installed as interim co-CEOs of Waystar Royco, travel with Shiv and the senior executive team to Norway for Lukas Matsson’s GoJo retreat, where they are meant to finalize the sale Logan had been pursuing before his death. Matsson destabilizes the talks by demanding ATN as part of the acquisition, while the Waystar staff worry about GoJo’s rumored post-deal layoffs. Shiv, excluded from the brothers’ formal arrangement, finds a private opening with Matsson after he confesses his harassment of Ebba. Roman’s grief and anger surface during a mountaintop confrontation, where he tells Matsson the brothers plan to kill the deal. The episode ends with Matsson bypassing them by sending Frank a huge offer for all of Waystar, including ATN, while asking Shiv to send him a photo of Kendall and Roman receiving the news.

## A Takeover Song in the Back Seat

“Kill List” opens by giving Kendall Roy exactly the image he wants and then leaving him inside it long enough for the image to sour. He rides in the back of a limo with Jay-Z’s “Takeover” blasting, enjoying the private ceremony of having finally reached the chair. The scene is funny because Kendall has always needed music, costume, jargon, and velocity to complete the idea of himself. The song does not accompany power. It supplies the sensation of it.

The limo matters because it is a sealed space. Kendall can arrange the soundtrack, inhabit the posture, and imagine the world outside the windows as already conquered. Nothing in the scene resists him because no one else has entered it yet. The episode then spends its running time testing that private composition against public rooms, foreign terrain, and other people’s appetites. Kendall can set the terms of his own entrance. He cannot make Norway hear the same song.

That difference between self-image and social recognition gives “Kill List” its shape. The episode is about negotiation, but its deeper subject is visibility: who gets looked at, who controls the frame, who becomes a specimen, who is kept outside the decisive conversation. Kendall and Roman arrive as interim co-CEOs, but the trip is already organized around Matsson’s gaze. He has requested the senior Waystar group, which means the brothers must bring the old regime with them like adult supervision. Frank, Karl, Gerri, Karolina, Hugo, Tom, Greg, and Shiv all travel not simply as support but as evidence that the new kings cannot yet stand alone.

Gerri understands the danger before the brothers do. On the plane, she delivers her “raised by wolves” speech, rallying Waystar’s veterans against GoJo’s younger, fitter, European staff. The joke depends on her accuracy. Waystar people have been trained by uglier conditions. They have lived under Logan Roy’s temper, learned to read fear as weather, and converted insult into a professional reflex. Gerri names that history without dressing it up. They are not noble survivors. They are creatures formed by exposure.

But the speech also shows how defensive the old Waystar imagination has become. It turns brutality into a credential because that is the only credential these people trust. Once they land, the fantasy of wolfish advantage immediately becomes unstable. The GoJo retreat does not look like a boardroom battlefield. It looks airy, scenic, ritualized, and annoyingly healthy: ferries, cabins, outdoor equipment, clean surfaces, expensive informality, meals that make the Waystar party unsure how to perform appetite. The landscape has a quiet tactical force. It makes the Roys and their entourage look overdressed even when they are dressed down.

Matsson’s control is social before it is financial. He does not simply host them. He places them. He lets them cross water, walk through open air, stand against vast views, and keep discovering that there is no familiar corner from which to dominate the room. The retreat absorbs corporate procedure into leisure rituals: eating, mingling, walking, drinking, talking near cliffs. In those settings the Waystar people cannot hide behind polished meeting architecture. Hugo’s overloaded plate, Tom’s strained networking, Greg’s gawky sexual-tourist patter, Roman’s complaints about the rooms, Kendall’s managerial brightness, all become visible as behaviors under inspection.

The title’s kill list begins as a rumor about layoffs after GoJo acquires Waystar. It gives every senior executive a reason to scan every smile for threat. Yet the episode widens the phrase until it applies to perception itself. People are being sorted by how they look in another culture’s frame. The Waystar old guard thought Logan had trained them to survive any hostile room. In Norway, hostility arrives as hospitality.

## ATN and the Asset That Cannot Stay Clean

Matsson’s surprise demand for ATN changes the negotiation because it changes what the brothers think is being measured. Until then, the sale can still be treated as the deal Logan began, a difficult but inherited transaction. ATN is different. It carries Logan’s voice more directly than the rest of the company. It is where appetite turns into programming, where political instinct becomes broadcast rhythm, where the Roy family’s idea of influence has its most visible machinery.

Kendall and Roman respond as if Matsson has touched a body part. Their resistance is partly financial, but the episode keeps returning it to grief, loyalty, and humiliation. Logan wanted to keep ATN. That fact gives the asset a devotional charge for the sons, even though neither of them can cleanly articulate why retaining it would make them stronger. The channel has become a relic, and relics are hard to price because their value depends on who is looking.

The episode refuses to let that reverence stand uncontaminated. When it cuts back to the ATN newsroom, Mencken’s presence is already in the air. The candidate’s people are not outside the glass, hoping for favorable treatment from a distance. They are near enough to shape conversation, close enough that editorial judgment has the feel of coordination. The asset Matsson wants is therefore not a pure inheritance under threat from an alien buyer. It is already morally compromised, already bent toward a political arrangement that the network has learned to treat as normal operating procedure.

That makes Shiv’s view of ATN sharper than her brothers’. She can call it toxic because she does not need it to hold Logan’s aura intact. Her clarity does not make her disinterested. She has her own resentments and calculations. Still, she sees the channel as a liability where Kendall and Roman see an altar. The difference matters because Matsson’s demand is also a test of who can describe the object accurately. He can afford to sound crude about it because he has no pious attachment to the Roy mythology. He sees a media weapon, a bargaining chip, and a weakness in the brothers’ posture.

The first negotiation with Matsson stages this imbalance through discomfort rather than grand confrontation. The setting is too beautiful for the conversation, which makes the men in it look small and fussy. Matsson sits with a looseness that feels almost rude. Kendall and Roman try to produce the cadence of adult dealmakers, but the rhythm keeps slipping. Matsson breaks the inherited script by insisting on ATN, then watches them react. He does not need to prove mastery with elaborate technique. He changes the subject, lets silence sit, and allows their irritation to become information.

This is where his power becomes tactile. He is broad, casual, physically unhurried. He drinks, jokes, wanders, withdraws, reappears. He handles the meeting less like a formal bidder than a host who knows everyone has to accept the terrain he has chosen. The Roys are used to Logan’s coercive rooms, where force moved through fear and interruption. Matsson’s pressure is different. It works through social imbalance. He makes them feel hosted, observed, and slightly late to a party whose rules everyone else understands.

Roman feels this with particular intensity. He has been the most openly damaged by the narrow timing of Logan’s death and the trip. The family has barely had time to understand the body as a body. Connor’s texts about Logan’s funeral arrangements turn that unresolved fact into an intrusion. When Roman receives the photo of Logan, the image punctures whatever professional shell he has managed to maintain. It is a brutal little collision of frames: the father reduced to a photograph for approval, the son standing in a foreign negotiation landscape, the dead man’s corporate deal still moving as if the body were an inconvenience.

Roman’s later outburst at Matsson comes from that collision. He accuses him of dragging out the sale, stressing Logan, summoning them too soon, and treating the grief as a business obstacle. The accusation has a childish simplicity and an adult accuracy at once. Matsson has exploited timing. Roman also needs someone to blame for the intolerable fact that Logan is no longer available to punish, bless, or define the room.

The mountaintop confrontation gives Roman too much space. There is no office wall to contain him, no familiar corporate furniture to let the performance settle into genre. He tells Matsson that he and Kendall intend to tank the deal, then half-protects himself by saying he can call it a tactic, then withdraws even that cover. The scene has the awkward force of someone trying to stop being false and discovering that truth is still usable against him. Roman thinks plain speech might break the frame. Matsson receives it as leverage.

## The People Who Know They Are Being Watched

Tom and Greg’s material in “Kill List” plays as comedy, but the comedy is exact. They know, more than the Roy siblings, that survival depends on being read correctly by strangers. Tom has no family name to convert into authority. Greg has almost no authority at all. Their scenes at the retreat show men trying to become acceptable under observation.

Tom approaches Matsson’s circle with the strained cheer of someone who understands that a new owner could erase him with no emotional cost. When the conversation turns to France, he tries to bluff a geopolitical position and immediately begins to sink. His face does a familiar Wambsgans calculation: charm, panic, deference, improvisation. Greg’s rescue is ridiculous, a stammering defense of “Old Lady France” in which the baguette somehow becomes mightier than the bagel, but it gives Tom a way out by redistributing the embarrassment. Greg absorbs the room’s laughter because absorbing laughter is one of his professional uses.

The exchange is minor only in plot terms. It clarifies a central difference in how characters handle exposure. Tom and Greg are humiliated, but humiliation gives them information. They learn who laughs, who listens, who tolerates them, who might still use them. Kendall and Roman, by contrast, experience misrecognition as an offense against their rightful image. They do not adjust quickly because adjustment would require admitting that their titles have not settled the question of how they appear.

Shiv’s route through the episode is more dangerous because she recognizes the frame and then uses it. Excluded from the brothers’ formal leadership, she becomes available to Matsson precisely because she is outside their official performance. He can speak to her without granting authority to the co-CEOs, and she can listen without pretending to defend their vanity. Their rapport begins in mutual appraisal. He sees that she resents being sidelined. She sees that he enjoys making the brothers visible as effortful men.

Their private exchange about Ebba and the frozen blood gives the episode its ugliest comic object. Matsson tells Shiv that he has sent his own blood to Ebba, GoJo’s head of communications and his former intimate partner. The detail is absurd, but it is not ornamental. It exposes the body beneath Matsson’s sleek social control. His power depends on informality, transgression, and the charm of not seeming bound by ordinary limits, but here that freedom has curdled into harassment so literal that it cannot be smoothed into language. Blood in bags, frozen and sent to an employee, is a fact too physical for brand management.

Shiv’s response is revealingly practical. She does not treat the confession as a moral shock that ends the conversation. She treats it as a liability problem with sexual, reputational, and financial dimensions. That is why Matsson keeps talking to her. She can look directly at the grotesque thing and translate it into consequence. He offers her a secret, but he also places her inside his own compromised frame. If she uses the access, she accepts some proximity to the dirt.

The Ebba conversation also revises Matsson’s apparent superiority. He is not clean, rational, or futuristically evolved beyond Waystar’s rot. He is simply better, in this episode, at deciding when other people must look and when they must not. Ebba’s strained presence at the retreat becomes part of that arrangement. She is visible enough to be a problem, not empowered enough to control the story around her. Matsson’s world is not free of humiliation. It has its own forms of exposure, disguised by better scenery and looser clothes.

Shiv’s advantage is that she can live, for a while, in the side channel. Sarah Snook plays these scenes with a guarded alertness, letting Shiv’s contempt remain present without letting it spoil the opportunity. She has been pushed out of the photograph Kendall and Roman want to take of themselves as co-CEOs. Matsson gives her a different line of sight. It is not liberation. It is access, and access is often what Shiv mistakes for movement.

## The Photograph After the Offer

The final call is a clean piece of dramatic cruelty because it turns Kendall and Roman’s defeat into something they have to smile near. Matsson sends Frank an offer of $192 per share for all of Waystar, ATN included. The price is high enough to make refusal look perverse. The board will like it. The senior executives can taste their exits and payouts. Karl, especially, receives the news with the lightness of a man whose private interests have suddenly become public virtue.

For Kendall and Roman, the offer produces a specific humiliation. They wanted the deal to die, but they needed its death to look like Matsson’s failure. Instead, he creates a number so attractive that their sabotage reads as successful negotiation. They are trapped inside the wrong interpretation. Everyone around them can congratulate the result because the market language says victory. Only they and Shiv know how badly the scene has misnamed them.

That misnaming is the episode’s real final turn. Matsson does not merely beat the brothers on terms. He asks Shiv for a photo of them as they absorb the news. The request is childish, predatory, and socially precise. He wants proof of their faces. He wants the image of the men he has forced into a celebration they cannot enjoy. He wants Shiv, the excluded sibling, to supply it.

The act of photographing completes the episode’s chain of visibility. Kendall begins by staging himself in a limo with a takeover anthem. Gerri tries to describe Waystar’s people before GoJo can classify them. Mencken’s shadow inside ATN reveals that the supposedly treasured asset has already entered someone else’s political frame. Connor’s photo of Logan breaks Roman’s professional mask by making the dead father suddenly, horribly viewable. Tom and Greg survive a social misfire by letting embarrassment move from one body to another. Matsson tells Shiv about blood he should never have sent and asks her to manage the visibility of that fact. At the end, Kendall and Roman become the image.

Shiv sending the photo is a small betrayal, but its scale suits the episode. “Kill List” is not built around a single operatic reversal. It works through pauses, glances, side conversations, and tiny acts of framing. The photograph does not change the offer. It records the new arrangement around it. Matsson has the number, Frank has the call, Shiv has the private channel, and the brothers have the faces someone else wants to see.
