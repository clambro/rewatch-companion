## Episode Summary

“With Open Eyes” brings the series to its final Waystar-GoJo board vote and the Roy siblings’ last attempt to keep the company in the family. Kendall and Shiv travel to Caroline’s house in Barbados to find Roman, who is bruised and stitched after the funeral and protest. Greg uses a translation app to learn that Matsson will not make Shiv CEO, then passes the information to Kendall. The siblings briefly align around Kendall as their candidate, anointing him in Caroline’s kitchen with a revolting smoothie. Back in New York, Roman falters after seeing Gerri and nearly breaks down before the vote. In the boardroom, Shiv withdraws her support, cites Kendall’s role in the waiter’s death, and casts the deciding vote for the sale. Tom is appointed CEO. The episode ends in quiet aftermath: Shiv and Tom ride together in a new arrangement, Kendall sits alone near the river under Colin’s watch, Roman drinks by himself at a bar, and Connor and Willa settle into a practical distance over Logan’s apartment and a possible posting abroad.

## The Last Image of Family

The finale gives the Roy siblings one more image of unity, and for a while it almost works. It moves them into Caroline’s house, away from the office floors, glass rooms, and aircraft cabins where most of their planning has taken place. Barbados is not a place of innocence, and Caroline is not a rescuing parent, but the house changes the temperature. The children appear in a domestic space where their competitive language loses some of its professional casing. They bicker, joke, raid a kitchen, and briefly become siblings before they become shareholders again.

The home video of Logan at dinner is the episode’s sharper version of the same temptation. Kendall, Shiv, Roman, Connor, and the others watch Logan in a register the series has rarely allowed them to hold for long: warm, funny, socially alive. He sits with Connor, Kerry, Karl, Gerri, Frank, and other people from the Roy orbit, relaxed enough to enjoy song, imitation, and vulgar wit. The scene does not redeem him or suggest that the children have misunderstood a lovable man. Its force comes from placement. The children encounter a father who seems to have had access to ease in rooms where they were not central.

The trap is scarcity. The Roy children have built their memory of Logan around a look, a phrase, a strategic invitation, a punishment that counted as attention. In the video, Logan appears capable of ordinary sociability with people who are not asking him to name an heir. He is still the same man, surrounded by employees and dependents who know how to please him, but the atmosphere is lighter than the one his children usually received. Logan did not lack warmth in some simple biological sense. He distributed it. The children’s private myth of him depended on imagining that his approval was difficult because it was rare in him altogether. The video suggests something worse: he could create conviviality when he did not feel succession pressing against him.

Against that unresolved family history, the kitchen scene offers a brief substitute for agreement. The disgusting smoothie becomes a counterfeit coronation because it uses childish materials to stage a corporate transfer. Bread, milk, tabasco, spit, whatever else they can find: the ingredients matter less than the form. They make Kendall kneel into a joke and then treat the joke as a blessing. Their intimacy has always moved most freely through insult and desecration; even tenderness needs a procedural shape. They cannot simply agree that Kendall is their candidate. They must turn agreement into a ceremony that disguises the violence of selection.

The kitchen scene is one of the rare late-series moments in which the three younger Roys seem able to enjoy one another, but the pleasure depends on suspension. No one is yet required to live under Kendall. Roman can give assent because it is still a joke, Shiv can allow it because Matsson has just humiliated her, and Kendall can receive it because the room has no immediate consequences. The instant the choice becomes operative, the old grammar returns.

## Domestic Memory and Corporate Procedure

Greg’s translation-app discovery is comic in method and decisive in effect. Matsson’s offhand Swedish conversation, caught by a subordinate with a phone, becomes the fact that rearranges the siblings’ strategy. Greg survives because he stands near doors, lingers after insults, hears what confident people assume he cannot use. Here his awkwardness and technical improvisation convert corporate chatter into leverage.

The family knows how to treat information as a weapon before it knows what it wants to do with it. Shiv’s position with Matsson collapses as soon as the promised CEO role vanishes. Her work for the deal, her posture of access, even her belief that she had outmaneuvered her brothers, are exposed as temporary usefulness. Kendall does not need to persuade her through a coherent vision of Waystar. He needs to show that Matsson has made her look like an instrument. The siblings’ alliance forms around injury before it forms around policy.

The boardroom feels less like a business scene than the final translation of childhood into procedure. Kendall enters the room with the force of someone who believes the ceremony has already certified him. He has the votes, or thinks he does. He sits in Logan’s chair earlier with the careless appetite of a man trying the body of power on before the room has agreed to see it. The chair is not magic; it does not transform him. It reveals how quickly he confuses access to the symbol with possession of the authority that once made the symbol intimidating.

Roman’s body disrupts the fantasy before Shiv does. His stitches, bruises, and hesitant movements carry the previous episode into this one. He has already failed to perform command over Logan’s coffin and over the street outside the funeral. In the office, when he sees Gerri, the private circuit through which he once converted humiliation into intimacy no longer works. He cannot make a joke stable enough to hide inside. His wound becomes visible evidence that he is not ready to submit to the next ceremony, even if he can still utter the required vote.

Kendall’s handling of Roman before the meeting is one of the episode’s sharpest bodily choices. The embrace can be read by the room as fraternal reassurance, but it also presses Roman’s injured face into Kendall’s shoulder. The gesture is deniable, intimate, and coercive. Kendall does not become Logan in some complete sense. He borrows one of Logan’s practical discoveries: pain administered inside a socially acceptable gesture can discipline the person receiving it.

The vote converts family language into procedure and then back into family injury. Frank, Karl, Gerri, Stewy, Sandy, Sandi, and the rest of the board reduce decades of Roy myth to counted positions. The room does what rooms like this do: it asks for a yes or a no. That simplicity is unbearable to the siblings because their entire lives have been built around reversible speech, conditional loyalty, and recognition delayed just long enough to remain useful. A tied vote leaves Shiv with the cleanest procedural burden in the series. She cannot triangulate around it. She must choose.

Her reversal is often easiest to describe as betrayal, but the scene is more severe than that. Shiv does not suddenly discover Kendall’s flaws. She has known them. What changes is that the vote makes her responsible for installing him. In Barbados she could anoint him because the act remained half-play. In the boardroom she would have to make him real. Her statement that she does not think he would be good at the job is plain, almost disappointingly plain, and that is why it cuts through the scene. She does not defeat him with a brilliant tactic. She refuses to certify him.

Kendall’s response exposes the depth of his need. When Shiv invokes the waiter’s death, he first tries to evade, then recasts, then denies the confession that once bound the siblings in the Tuscan dirt. The denial is not primarily a legal maneuver. It is a desperate attempt to remove from the room any fact that would make his coronation impossible. In season three, the confession worked because Kendall had reached a point where being held by his siblings mattered more than being plausible. In the finale, plausibility matters again, so the confession must become a story, then a tactic, then nothing.

Kendall wants the office to certify him so completely that even shared reality must yield to it. His shout about being the eldest boy is childish in wording and precise in psychology. He does not argue like a CEO. He argues like a son trying to enforce an old promise whose authority depends on everyone forgetting Connor and accepting Kendall’s private version of Logan’s intention. When Roman attacks the legitimacy of Kendall’s children in Logan’s eyes, the scene reaches the family’s ugliest register: bloodline, inheritance, and insult fused into one final bid to define who counts.

The shove that follows is the scene’s proof. Kendall cannot endure refusal from the only witnesses whose certification would make the role feel real. Shiv and Roman can coordinate with him when coordination has no single bearer of consequence. They can make a smoothie, laugh in a kitchen, and rush through headquarters as a bloc. They cannot make one of themselves king and then live under him. The Roy children can inherit pieces of Logan’s force, but not the conditions that made that force work.

## Tom and the Uses of Humiliation

Tom’s appointment as CEO is neither an underdog triumph nor a clean reward for patience. Matsson chooses him because Tom is useful, adaptive, and absorbent. He can be placed between the owner and the institution. He can take contempt without insisting that contempt be converted into respect. His phrase about being a pain sponge is comic because it is accurate. Tom understands that the next version of Waystar does not need a sovereign heir. It needs an American interface willing to suffer, execute, flatter, and remain replaceable.

That makes him the finale’s answer to the succession question, though not in the way Kendall imagines the question. The show’s final test is not who most resembles Logan in voice, appetite, or cruelty. Each of the children can imitate some fragment of him. Kendall can stage scale and command a room for short bursts. Shiv can cut precisely and turn personal knowledge into tactical advantage. Roman can use humiliation as contact and diagnose fraudulence faster than anyone around him. None of them can endure the indignities of service without needing those indignities to become proof of specialness.

Tom can. His victory comes from a lower, colder competence. He has spent the series being mocked by Shiv, used by Logan, threatened by prison, and made ridiculous in front of people whose approval he wanted. He learned to keep moving. He also learned to pass humiliation downward, especially through Greg, which prevents any sentimental reading of his rise. The sticker he places on Greg after the betrayal is not forgiveness in a generous sense. It is ownership, punishment, and retention at once. Tom keeps Greg because Greg remains useful, and because their bond has always been a chain disguised as intimacy.

The contrast with Kendall is exact. Kendall needs the title to tell him who he is. Tom can accept that the title tells everyone what function he performs. Kendall wants the room to recognize destiny. Tom lets Matsson define the job as service and takes it anyway. He wins because he can be absorbed.

Shiv’s final position beside him is correspondingly unsentimental. She has blocked Kendall, injured him, protected herself from installing a brother she cannot stomach, and delivered the company to a husband whose new authority depends on another man’s ownership. The car scene refuses the satisfaction of either reconciliation or punishment. Tom extends his hand. Shiv places hers on it with a gesture so slight it almost looks administrative. Their marriage has become an arrangement with a visible hierarchy, but not a simple inversion. She is not erased; he is not secure in any deep sense. The hand-hold marks a contract whose emotional content remains unresolved.

## Silence After the Vote

The ending lets silence do the labor that earlier scenes gave to argument. After the boardroom, the episode does not ask Kendall, Shiv, Roman, or Tom to explain themselves. Logan’s chair, Roman’s stitches, the kitchen smoothie, Greg’s phone, Tom’s open palm, Kendall’s bench by the river: the finale closes through objects and bodies rather than speeches.

Roman’s bar scene is one of the episode’s cleanest refusals of catharsis. He has lost the company, but the scene does not frame him as newly liberated in any stable way. His faint smile and drink register resignation more than release. He has named the contest as empty, and that clarity leaves him with less to perform. For Roman, the succession fight gave shape to his cruelty, his jokes, and his need for punishment. Without it, he is not healed. He is simply outside the arena that made those habits look purposeful.

Kendall’s final image is more isolated but should not be inflated beyond what the show gives. He walks to the water with Colin behind him, the old apparatus of protection still present and useless. He sits near the river, looking out, unable to convert defeat into performance because there is no room left to address. The shot holds him in contemplation, not release. His claim that he was built as a cog for one machine is both self-pitying and accurate enough to sting. The machine has been sold, and the role he imagined as destiny has been filled by a man who never needed destiny to be part of the job description.

The title, “With Open Eyes,” does not announce enlightenment. The characters see more than they can use. Shiv sees Kendall clearly enough to stop him and Tom clearly enough to know what she has entered. Roman sees the contest’s emptiness but has no life ready outside it. Kendall sees the office vanish and cannot turn that knowledge into another self. Tom sees the terms of his usefulness and accepts them. Connor, peripheral as ever, sees that arrangements can hold if one stops demanding that they become proof of importance.

The episode closes the series without making the sale feel like a grand moral verdict. Waystar passes out of family control, but the deeper pattern remains intact. The Roy children were trained to mistake resemblance for inheritance: a voice, a chair, a cruelty, a capacity to wound. What they could not inherit was Logan’s ability to make others live inside his chosen reality before the vote was even taken. “With Open Eyes” gives them one last room in which they appear, almost convincingly, as a family. Then it places them in a room where appearance must become consequence. That is where the image fails.
