The finale argues that “With Open Eyes” is less a twist than a final exposure of the Roy family’s governing logic: the siblings can still produce fleeting rituals of unity, but those rituals only work while choice remains theatrical and consequence is suspended. Caroline’s house and the grotesque smoothie coronation create a temporary domestic vocabulary in which Kendall can be anointed, while the home video of Logan at dinner complicates the family’s myth by showing that he could distribute warmth and conviviality when succession was not in play, making his children’s scarcity-based memory of him look self-serving and incomplete. But the episode’s real structure is the conversion of intimacy into procedure, as Greg’s translation-app intelligence, Roman’s bodily injury, and Shiv’s humiliation by Matsson push the siblings toward the board vote, where Kendall’s need for certification collides with Shiv’s refusal to make him real and Roman’s inability to sustain the old family circuit of humiliation as intimacy. Kendall’s claim to be the eldest boy and his handling of Roman through a coercive hug show him borrowing Logan’s disciplinary methods without inheriting Logan’s authority over rooms, while Shiv’s decisive reversal is framed not as sudden discovery but as the unbearable responsibility of installing a brother she cannot trust. Tom’s victory is the episode’s cold answer to succession, since he can absorb humiliation, serve as an interface, and accept being useful rather than sovereign, unlike the Roy children, who need power to confirm identity. The ending’s silence, with Roman drinking alone, Kendall isolated by the river, Shiv and Tom entering a contract-like marriage, and Connor settling for practical arrangements, confirms the central judgment: the Roys can inherit pieces of Logan’s force, but not the conditions that made that force stick.
