## Episode Summary

“The Munsters,” season 4, episode 1 of _Succession_, opens the final season after a six-month jump from the end of season 3. Kendall, Shiv, and Roman are in Los Angeles preparing an investor pitch for their new media venture, The Hundred, when a phone call from Tom alerts Shiv that Logan is trying to buy Pierce Global Media. The siblings abandon the pitch, visit Nan Pierce, and push their offer from $8 billion to $10 billion, beating Logan’s bid. In New York, Logan’s birthday party unfolds without Kendall, Shiv, or Roman; Connor worries about his presidential campaign polling at 1 percent, Greg’s date draws unwanted attention, and Tom tries to secure his own future if his marriage ends. Shiv later returns to her apartment, finds Tom there, and agrees to move toward divorce without the conversation Tom wants. The episode ends with Logan, after losing Pierce, sitting alone on the couch watching ATN and calling Cyd Peach to complain about the broadcast.

## The LA Pitch and the Pierce Pivot

The first Los Angeles scenes let the siblings look briefly like people who might have found a usable form for one another. They sit together over The Hundred, tuning phrases, testing the pitch, trying to sound like founders rather than heirs. The project’s language is ridiculous in a recognizably expensive way: a premium information product, elite writers, “high-calorie” knowledge, a nonprofit ethos with a path to aggressive margins. It sounds less like a company than a wish to be thought modern, serious, and untainted by Waystar.

The show does not need anyone to call the idea hollow. It lets the room do the work. The siblings are surrounded by the light and glass of a West Coast beginning, but the vocabulary keeps dragging them back to old habits: scale, prestige, acquisition, a market position big enough to prove they have not been diminished. Kendall likes the sweep of it. Shiv likes the strategic sheen. Roman keeps worrying over execution, partly because he is the one most alert to how quickly their pitch can become another fantasy dressed in business language. The Hundred asks them to build something with patience. The episode is already preparing the moment when patience loses.

That moment arrives through Tom’s phone call. Shiv hears that Tom has met with Naomi Pierce, and the information immediately changes the temperature of the LA plot. Logan is pursuing Pierce again. A company he has long coveted is back in play. The siblings do not merely identify an opportunity. They recognize an object bearing Logan’s desire, and that recognition makes The Hundred feel suddenly small. The episode’s cross-cutting between Los Angeles and New York sharpens the shift: while the children rehearse independence in bright rooms, Logan’s birthday gathers in darker interiors, heavy with old employees, family residue, and expectation. The supposed new beginning is edited against the old center until the new beginning gives way.

Roman resists the pivot with more sense than the others want to grant him. He sees the practical incoherence of launching a “high-visibility” media start-up while also trying to buy a legacy-media company that will require money, attention, and political exposure. He also sees the emotional shape of the move. Kendall wants to hit Logan. Shiv wants to hit Logan and Tom. Roman understands that a bid can be a tantrum with bankers attached. His objections matter because they show that the siblings are not blindly confused about what they are doing. They can name the trap and still walk into it.

The Pierce scenes then turn valuation into a family message. The siblings begin at $8 billion, a number large enough to be taken seriously and low enough to suggest they still have some discipline. Nan Pierce’s hesitation, Naomi’s mediation, and the continual sense that Logan is moving somewhere offscreen push them upward. Roman suspects Nan is playing them. Kendall and Shiv prefer the relief of decisiveness. By the time they land on $10 billion, the amount has stopped functioning as a careful price. It is a sentence directed at Logan: we can take what you want.

Shiv’s call to Tom gives the sequence its cleanest mechanics. She cannot get to Logan directly, so she uses the damaged marriage as a channel into his bid. Tom, anxious about his status and still entangled with her, becomes a relay. The episode keeps the business action intimate without softening it. Shiv is not simply gathering market intelligence. She is turning the one person who betrayed her into an instrument against the man he chose. Tom knows enough to be guarded, not enough to stay opaque. The call belongs to the same world as the later apartment scene, where marriage remains emotionally legible but operationally exhausted.

When Nan accepts the $10 billion offer, the siblings get the kind of win they understand best: public, expensive, witnessed, and defined by Logan’s loss. Their alignment has worked, but the form of that success exposes its limit. The Hundred required shared belief in a future outside Logan’s frame. Pierce gives them a way back into that frame under the appearance of independence. They are together, yet the thing that binds them is still his appetite.

The title “The Munsters” hovers over this structure with a light touch. The episode does not need to force a joke about monstrous rich people. The resemblance matters more than the gag. The Roys keep arranging themselves into domestic shapes: birthday party, sibling project, awkward guest, marital encounter, campaign anxiety, father in front of the television. The shapes are recognizable. The household logic inside them remains grotesque. Even after the succession contest has supposedly ended, the children reproduce the family pattern by finding a new object through which to fight for recognition.

## Logan’s Birthday Without His Children

Logan’s birthday party is staged as a social event that no longer knows how to honor its center. People circulate through the home, but the party has little warmth and not much pressure. That absence is new. Earlier gatherings around Logan often throbbed with danger because the children needed something from him in the room. Here Kendall, Shiv, and Roman are elsewhere, and Connor’s attendance cannot supply the missing charge. Logan still possesses the house, the staff, the executives, the guests. What has thinned is the feeling that the room reorganizes itself through filial need.

The episode keeps returning to surfaces of manners. Greg brings Bridget, a date who does not understand the rules of the space. Her handbag, her comfort with food, her posting, and her general lack of trained discretion become small scandals because Logan’s home depends on invisible protocols. She is not dangerous in any ordinary sense. She is embarrassing, and embarrassment in this world quickly becomes a security matter. Tom’s management of Greg and Bridget is comic in phrasing but exact in function. He polices the boundary between those who know how to disappear properly and those who expose the party as a performance.

Tom is enforcing rules at the same time he is unsure whether the rules will protect him. He has backed Logan, betrayed Shiv, and remained near the winning side of season 3’s final move. Yet when he asks Logan what might happen if he and Shiv separate, Logan gives him no firm assurance. The exchange is brief, but it clarifies Tom’s position at the start of the final season. He has purchased proximity through betrayal, and proximity has not become safety. Logan lets him remain useful without allowing him to feel secure.

Connor’s subplot sits in the same party atmosphere, apparently marginal and quietly revealing. His presidential campaign is polling at 1 percent, and he worries about being squeezed from that number. The comedy is almost mathematical: Connor clings to a figure that is both negligible and sacred. One percent is not power, but it is a public measurement, and public measurement gives him a role. His talk of spending another immense sum to preserve that role, and of turning the wedding to Willa into a campaign spectacle, follows the pattern he has built across the series. When he cannot receive importance from the family, he manufactures ceremonies that might certify it from the outside.

The episode does not treat Connor as merely deluded. His position has its own grim clarity. He knows what it means to be the child who is present and still not central. The wedding, the campaign, the polling number, the possible media event around Willa all become props in an arrangement that might hold because it asks less from Logan than the other children ask. Connor wants attention, but he does not want the company. His bargain is weaker than theirs and in some ways more livable.

Logan’s departure from his own party to eat with Colin supplies the episode’s starkest contrast to the LA bid. In Los Angeles, the children perform corporate futurity. In New York, Logan sits with the one man whose loyalty has been professionally defined. Colin is safe company because he cannot demand the kind of recognition Logan withholds from family. Their dinner has an odd plainness: Logan talks about people as economic units, about death, about the possibility that nothing follows. The scene does not sentimentalize him. It shows how limited his vocabulary becomes when the room stops fighting him.

That limitation is the point. Logan can speak of human beings as values, prices, markets, and uses, but he still wants the kind of attachment that cannot survive those terms. He wants his children defeated and near. He wants to sell the company and remain the father whose desires define the game. He wants the party without the vulnerability of being celebrated. The episode makes those contradictions spatial before it makes them explicit. The full house feels empty. The private dinner feels less intimate than logistical. Colin can sit across from him, but Colin cannot answer the old family hunger that Logan himself has trained into competition.

When Logan returns and learns that the children have moved on Pierce, their absence becomes active. They are not at the party, but they have found the one remaining way to enter the room: by bidding against him. The episode revises the end of season 3 in this precise way. Logan won the structural fight by cutting them out of the GoJo sale. He did not free himself from them. They can still reach him through the market, especially when the market contains something he wants.

His insult after the loss, congratulating them for saying the biggest number, is both accurate and defensive. The siblings may have overpaid. They may also have beaten him to the thing he wanted. Logan’s contempt cannot quite erase the fact that he has been touched. The birthday party has failed to gather his children, but the Pierce auction has summoned them in another form. They return as bidders because they cannot return as heirs.

## Shiv and Tom at the Apartment

The apartment scene between Shiv and Tom narrows the episode’s public contests into a private room. Shiv arrives to collect clothes and finds Tom there. The space already feels divided, less like a home than a location being processed after use. They do not stage a fight. Tom asks for a conversation. Shiv tries to keep the separation clean, calm, and procedural.

The difference between them is not that Tom feels and Shiv does not. The scene is more exact than that. Tom wants the marriage to become narratable. He wants to explain, revisit, account for what happened, perhaps put his betrayal and her contempt into some sequence that might still include feeling. Shiv resists the danger of that sequence. If the marriage becomes a story, then it contains wounds, claims, obligations, and moments that might have to be answered rather than managed. If it remains an arrangement ending by agreement, she can preserve a measure of control.

Sarah Snook and Matthew Macfadyen play the scene almost against melodrama. Tom’s softness has pressure inside it. Shiv’s composure keeps slipping toward sadness and then correcting itself. They sit near one another with enough history to make distance visible. The small contact near the end, their hands together for a moment, matters because it is neither false nor sufficient. The marriage is not empty. It has simply been trained into the same habits as the family business: terms, advantage, exposure, standing, the fear that need will be used by whoever names it first.

This is why the earlier Tom phone call belongs to the same episode design as the apartment scene. Shiv uses Tom as a route into Logan’s Pierce bid, and later Tom asks her for a route back into meaning. The two requests cannot coexist. In the world they inhabit, an emotional opening almost immediately becomes useful information. Tom has learned this lesson well enough to survive, but not well enough to stop wanting the marriage to be more than a structure of access. Shiv has learned it so thoroughly that even grief appears to her as something best handled by limiting the conversation.

The scene also places Tom at the beginning of the final season’s path. He has not yet become the acceptable, replaceable executive who will sit in the chair after Matsson’s deal closes. Here he is still trying to know whether the Roy connection he married into can protect him after the marriage itself fails. The answer is already visible. Shiv cannot protect him because she will not grant him that kind of dependence. Logan will not protect him except as long as he remains useful. Tom’s education is to stop confusing intimacy with shelter.

## The Couch and the Machine

“The Munsters” works as a season opener by placing the characters after the apparent end of the succession contest and then watching them recreate its pressures through other objects. The GoJo sale is near completion. The children have been cut out. The old question of which child will inherit Waystar has lost its first shape. Yet the episode does not need to reverse those facts to restore the drama. It only has to show how quickly the Roys answer when Logan’s desire enters the room.

The form of the episode carries that argument more cleanly than any declaration could. Los Angeles and New York are held apart, then made to speak to each other through phone calls, bids, and absences. The investor pitch and the birthday party are both performances, one directed toward a future and one toward a patriarchal ritual that has gone stale. The Hundred’s bright corporate language collapses into the Pierce auction. Logan’s crowded celebration collapses into dinner with Colin and then into a study where executives wait for news from the children. Public confidence and domestic emptiness keep cutting against each other.

The Pierce bid gives the siblings a victory, but not an exit. Their $10 billion offer proves they can move together when the target is clear and paternal. It does not prove they can sustain an independent project, manage a company with discipline, or stop measuring success by Logan’s reaction. Roman’s hesitation remains in the episode as the path they do not take. The children could have stayed with The Hundred, however absurd. They choose instead the contest that lets them feel most coherent because it gives them back the father as opponent.

Logan’s final image is smaller than the episode’s business scale. He sits alone on the couch watching ATN. He can still call Cyd Peach and complain about what he sees. The network remains close enough to his body that a broadcast annoyance becomes a command impulse. Yet the framing reduces him. He is not presiding over a room, not humiliating a table, not pulling his children into orbit by physical presence. He is a solitary viewer with the power to phone the machine, irritated by an image that no longer gives him adequate return. At the start of season 4, Logan still has command. The episode leaves him with command stripped down to a couch, a screen, and a call into the apparatus that outlasts his company.
