“The Munsters” argues that after the succession struggle appears to be settled, the Roys immediately convert independence into another mode of filial competition, using Logan’s desire as the real object of their choices. The Hundred initially lets Kendall, Shiv, and Roman seem briefly coherent as founders, but the episode shows their corporate futurity collapsing the moment Logan’s Pierce obsession reenters the frame; the siblings pivot from building something to outbidding him, and Roman’s practical objections expose the move as a tantrum with bankers attached. The Pierce purchase becomes a family message rather than a business decision, proof they can still reach Logan only by taking what he wants. Against this, the New York material shows a household whose rituals have gone hollow: Logan’s birthday party lacks the old filial charge, Greg’s social incompetence and Connor’s one percent campaign underline each character’s need for a recognizably public form of importance, and Logan’s dinner with Colin reveals his own dependence on controllable loyalty. The Shiv and Tom apartment scene narrows the episode’s emotional logic, linking marital collapse to the show’s larger economy of access, utility, and refused vulnerability. Overall, the episode uses cross-cutting, valuation, and domestic surfaces to show that the Roys do not escape Logan’s orbit after succession, but reproduce it, turning every new beginning into another bid for his attention.
