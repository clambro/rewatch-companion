## Episode Summary

“Honeymoon States” takes place in Logan Roy’s apartment during the wake after his death. The A-plot is the fight over interim control of Waystar while the GoJo deal remains unsettled. Frank, Karl, and Gerri discover a paper from Logan’s safe that appears to name Kendall as successor, though a pencil mark makes even that apparent wish unstable. Shiv privately learns her pregnancy is progressing, Connor bargains with Marcia to buy the apartment, and Tom and Greg search for protection now that Logan is gone. Kendall and Roman become co-CEOs, excluding Shiv from the formal role, and Kendall ends the episode by privately blackmailing Hugo into attacking Logan’s reputation without telling Roman.

## The Apartment Becomes the Company

“Honeymoon States” is a one-location chamber piece disguised as a wake. Logan’s apartment is converted, room by room, into three overlapping spaces: a place of mourning, a boardroom, and a family sorting room. The drama lies in watching people test where they can stand now that the man who organized every room has disappeared.

Huge windows fill the rooms with natural light, but the brightness does not clarify anything. It makes the maneuvering more exposed. The city sits outside the glass as an indifferent backdrop while inside the apartment people cluster, separate, listen, and recalibrate. When Logan was alive, people arranged themselves around his reaction even when he said little. Here, the same furniture remains, the same staff circulate, the same associates arrive, but the apartment has lost its center. The long table becomes a corporate table because no one can keep household ritual and corporate procedure apart. Authority has become something overheard.

The wake begins with the siblings in their briefest state of ease. Kendall, Shiv, Roman, and eventually Connor read Logan’s obituaries and translate public reverence into family knowledge. The memorial language cannot survive contact with what they know. “Complicated,” “man of his era,” and other polished phrases become material for their shared contempt. The laughter is real. It is one of the few scenes in which the siblings can occupy a common perspective without immediately trying to outrank one another.

The tenderness is not false. It simply has limits. The siblings can be unified while they are reading the world’s version of Logan against their own. They can agree that the obituary Logan is a fabrication. But the moment Logan’s private residue enters the room, the hierarchy returns. Their solidarity depends on no one having to accept a lower position. “Honeymoon States” turns that condition into a structure: first the shared joke, then the document, then the board vote, then Kendall’s private betrayal.

## The Paper From the Safe

The paper from Logan’s safe is the episode’s hinge because it seems to offer posthumous authorization and then fails to settle anything. It appears to name Kendall as Logan’s preferred successor, but the pencil mark across or under Kendall’s name makes the blessing unreadable. It is old, legally nonbinding, and emotionally decisive. Its force comes from the gap between those facts.

Frank, Karl, and Gerri understand the danger before the children see it. Their first instinct is containment. Karl wants to know whether this object has to become real. Gerri measures exposure. Frank, who retains a particular softness toward Kendall, cannot quite bury it. The scene is comic because of the practical cowardice in the room, and sharp about Waystar’s post-Logan condition. The old guard knows that a scrap of paper can become a weather system if the family believes it carries Logan’s will.

Once the siblings read it, everyone interprets the paper through need. Kendall sees the possibility of being chosen. Roman sees the return of a contest he had briefly escaped through grief. Shiv sees, correctly, the first instrument of her exclusion. The ambiguity of the underline or cross-out is not a puzzle the episode asks us to solve. Its function is to keep Logan’s supposed wish available to whoever can use it most aggressively.

Even the stray “Greg?” turns the document into a miniature of Logan’s governance. It contains something that might be succession planning, something that might be insult, something that might be a note to himself, and something that might be meaningless. Everyone has to behave as if intention may be hidden inside accident. That was Logan’s usual advantage while living: other people supplied coherence to his marks, silences, and changes of mood. In death, the paper makes them do it again.

The staircase scene after the document gives that shift a visible shape. Shiv, Roman, and Kendall are no longer side by side over obituary jokes. They are arranged on steps, caught between rooms, with rank reintroduced as architecture. Shiv’s question about whether the good feeling among them is already over lands because the previous good feeling was not a lie. They did share something after Logan’s death. They did protect one another for a short time from the loneliness of the event. But the paper converts grief back into succession, and succession is the language in which this family knows how to injure itself.

Kendall’s reaction is restrained by his standards, which makes it more telling. He does not explode into triumph. He asks whether the paper is real, studies the mark, and begins to adjust his claim around it. The object gives him something stronger than a title and weaker than proof: a possible paternal sentence. For Kendall, that is enough to alter the air. The board can appoint him, Stewy can support him, Roman can join him, but the paper supplies the form of recognition he has always wanted and never trusted.

## Exclusion, Bargains, and Temporary Shelter

The co-CEO arrangement looks clean only because Shiv is asked to absorb its mess. Kendall has the paper. Roman has recent operational proximity and an existing corporate role. Shiv has intelligence, fluency, and a claim as Logan’s daughter, but the brothers translate her lack of Waystar executive experience into a reason she must remain outside the formal office. Their argument is not wholly invented. That is what makes it useful.

The cruelty lies in the professional tone. Kendall and Roman do not stage a dramatic betrayal. They offer assurances. Shiv will be inside everything. The arrangement will be temporary. The optics need to look “clean” and “dry.” The old family move arrives in managerial language: define someone as less necessary, then ask her to recognize the definition as realism.

Shiv’s pregnancy sharpens that isolation in episode-specific terms. The doctor’s call comes before the wake, in a calm register that clashes with the day’s disorder. The pregnancy is progressing, a 20-week scan can be scheduled, and Shiv receives the information alone. No one in the apartment knows. Not her brothers, not Tom, not the people now asking her to trust an informal place beside two formally empowered men.

That secrecy changes the texture of her scenes without turning pregnancy into a general symbol. Shiv is already cut off from the office, from her marriage, and from a stable claim on Logan’s memory. The pregnancy gives her another private fact she cannot safely place anywhere. When Tom approaches her with a version of kindness, she hears the career maneuver underneath it. He is a man who backed the wrong protector and now reads the room for another patron. Yet the scene is not empty calculation. Tom remembers a courtship. Shiv recognizes both the feeling and the angle. Her withheld pregnancy makes their failed intimacy more precise: he is closer to her than almost anyone in the room and still has no access to the fact most immediately altering her day.

Tom’s movement through the apartment has the anxious politeness of a man trying doors. He approaches the old guard, then the brothers, then Shiv, registering each response as information. Karl’s warning reduces him to his current market value. Kendall’s mild “good luck” is worse than a threat because it offers no drama to work with. Tom has spent years making humiliation usable. Here he discovers that endurance does not automatically produce shelter when the patron who could cash it in has died.

Greg performs the apprentice version of the same scramble. He hovers near conversations, tries to attach himself to likely winners, and mistakes proximity for assignment. His awkwardness no longer reads as innocence. It is a method that has not yet become smooth. When the large players move, Greg inches after them, hoping to be included in the next arrangement before anyone remembers to exclude him.

Marcia, by contrast, enters already in possession. Her authority over the apartment is immediate because she understands the value of space, composure, and marital status. Her claim that she and Logan remained in intimate contact may be true, embellished, or strategically timed. What matters is that no one can disprove it quickly enough to dislodge her. She stands inside the home and makes others negotiate with that fact.

Her handling of Kerry is cold because it is administrative. Kerry arrives believing some private promise from Logan might still protect her. Marcia reduces that belief to logistics: the things are in a bag, the exit is not the front door, the car will take her away. Roman’s objection has weight because he recognizes a familiar Roy punishment. Kerry confused access with standing. Once Logan cannot enforce the difference in her favor, Marcia does.

Connor’s bargain with Marcia over the apartment is brief, funny, and clean. He wants Logan’s home because he cannot have Logan’s company or Logan’s central attention. Buying the apartment gives him a physical relation to significance. Marcia sees a buyer and closes quickly. The $63 million agreement is absurdly fast, but it is one of the episode’s clearest transactions. Connor’s life has often turned neglect into ceremony; here he turns it into real estate.

The honeymoon-states joke works the same way. Wisconsin, Minnesota, Michigan, Pennsylvania: the honeymoon becomes campaign routing. Connor and Willa’s marriage is transactional, but not therefore meaningless. They know the bargain more plainly than the siblings know theirs. Romance, property, campaign optics, and mourning fold into the same settlement.

## Kendall’s Private Turn

The board vote installs Kendall and Roman as co-CEOs, but the episode’s real ending comes after the public arrangement. Hugo and Karolina present two communications strategies. One preserves Logan as the great founder and promises continuity. The other uses his decline, cruelty, and failures to make the sons look like renewal. Roman rejects the second approach because attacking Logan so soon after death violates something he cannot translate into corporate language. Kendall appears to agree.

Then he privately orders Hugo to do it anyway.

The scene revises the co-CEO arrangement at once. Publicly, Kendall and Roman stand together. Privately, Kendall creates an asymmetry. He does not persuade Roman, consult Shiv, or trust the office he has just accepted. He isolates Hugo and uses Hugo’s exposure over his daughter’s stock sale after the premature leak of Logan’s death. The method is simple: identify the liability, make it personal, demand service, keep the order deniable.

This is Kendall’s sharpest act in the episode because it is not a performance for the room. It is quiet, procedural, and coercive. He does not yet command people the way Logan did. He cannot make the whole apartment reorganize around him by force of presence. But he can find a compromised subordinate and make that person obey. The distinction matters. Kendall has not become Logan. He has found one of Logan’s tools and used it quickly.

That final move clarifies the paper’s real effect. The possible blessing from the safe does not settle Kendall. It agitates him. The board vote does not satisfy him. Roman beside him is not enough, and Shiv’s reluctant consent is not enough. He needs a hidden advantage inside the shared structure, a private proof that he can act without permission. His first act as co-CEO is to make the co-CEO fiction untrue.

“Honeymoon States” ends not with succession secured but with succession contaminated at the moment of birth. The apartment has hosted mourning, bargaining, appointment, and betrayal in a single day. The siblings’ tenderness was real, but it could not survive the return of rank. The paper promised authorization and produced interpretation. Kendall and Roman emerge as the public face of continuity, while Kendall’s private order to Hugo shows what the new regime already contains: unity for display, secrecy underneath, and coercion presented as work.
