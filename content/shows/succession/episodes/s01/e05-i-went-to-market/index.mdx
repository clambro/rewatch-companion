## Episode Summary

“I Went to Market” brings the Roys together for Thanksgiving as Kendall quietly seeks support for a vote of no confidence against Logan. On the way to the Roy gathering, Tom phones about his prenup and tries to understand what protection it gives him. Greg drives Ewan Roy back from Canada, then gets sent by Tom to shred incriminating Waystar papers, while secretly making copies. The dinner becomes a battlefield of family cruelty and corporate positioning. Logan’s throwing of a can of cranberry sauce at Iverson is the episode’s final public escalation.

## Thanksgiving as a Market

Thanksgiving becomes an exchange. Affection, embarrassment, loyalty, access, and fear circulate through the apartment, but none of them has a stable price. A gesture of concern can become evidence. A joke can become a test. A family meal can become the same kind of room as a board meeting, only with better china and worse faith.

Kendall is building a coalition against Logan, but the show keeps placing that effort beside holiday logistics, family arrivals, seating arrangements, and household irritations. The editing lets the two plots contaminate each other. Kendall cannot keep the coup in the clean language of governance, because the people he needs are also dinner guests, relatives, employees, and veterans of Logan’s long discipline. A vote of no confidence sounds procedural. In this house, it requires people to acknowledge publicly what they have learned to manage in private.

Kendall must translate private feeling into institutional support. He needs others to turn worry about Logan’s health, disgust at his behavior, and fear of his judgment into a vote. A person can see Logan failing and still hesitate to oppose him. A person can recognize danger and still avoid becoming visible. Kendall has the mechanics of a challenge, but he does not yet have the authority to make hesitation harden into allegiance.

Thanksgiving gives the show the right room for that failure. Around the table, the family cannot keep domestic ritual separate from corporate threat. Business talk seeps into small talk. Small complaints acquire strategic weight. People interrupt, redirect, glance across the table, and measure whether a remark has landed safely. The long dinner sequence stages status without speeches. Who gets cut off matters. Who is allowed to continue after being cut off matters more. Silence does not mean neutrality; it often means someone is calculating the cost of reaction.

Logan’s power remains atmospheric here. He does not need full health, elegance, or even coherence to dominate the room. His presence alters the temperature. The table reorganizes around his attention, and the episode’s blocking keeps showing people watching him while pretending not to. His lapses are dangerous because everyone must decide how much to notice. His anger is dangerous because everyone already knows what it means to become its object.

## Tom’s Contract Fantasy

The opening Tom phone call is a dry prelude to the dinner. It looks like a small domestic embarrassment: Tom consults his mother, who is also his lawyer, about the prenup while heading toward Thanksgiving with Shiv’s family. But Tom is trying to prepare for marriage the way everyone else is trying to prepare for succession: by finding a contract that can protect him from dependence.

The fantasy collapses almost as soon as it appears. Tom wants the document to tell him where he stands, but standing with the Roys is not secured by a clause. A prenup can address money after divorce. It cannot guarantee dignity, equal footing, or the right to choose which Thanksgiving table he attends. Shiv’s small corrections and refusals in the car make that clear. Tom can ask whether the terms are fair, but fairness has little force inside a family that treats need as weakness and attachment as a bargaining position.

The scene catches Tom before he has fully learned how to survive this world. He is already deferential, already anxious, already aware that he has less protection than he wants. What he does not yet understand is that humiliation will become his education. Later, he will learn to absorb embarrassment, preserve usefulness, and attach himself to the strongest nearby power. Here, he still wants a document to save him from exposure. It cannot.

## Greg, Ewan, and the Value of Paper

Greg and Ewan’s drive gives the episode its strongest counterpoint to the dinner. Ewan is not Logan’s clean moral opposite. He is another Roy, hard in a different register, and he refuses the family’s sentimental language without becoming sentimental himself. His disgust with Logan is real, but it does not free him from Roy forms of command. He expects Greg’s time, obedience, and accommodation. His estrangement has not made him soft.

That distinction keeps the episode from turning Ewan into a clean corrective. He sees through the family mythology and has little patience for Logan’s luxury, appetites, or self-importance. Yet when Kendall approaches him about the vote, Ewan does not become the obvious ally Kendall needs. He may despise Logan, but he will not casually turn on his brother for Kendall’s benefit. Opposition to Logan does not automatically produce liberation from Logan’s terms.

Greg spends the episode being treated as stray property. Ewan needs a driver. Tom needs a subordinate to handle dangerous paperwork. The Roys need him to remain harmless, awkward, and grateful for proximity. The shredding assignment gives that condition a physical form. Greg misses the meal to destroy documents that might expose Waystar’s wrongdoing, doing menial work that is also legally perilous. He is useful because he is low enough to be sacrificed.

His decision to copy the papers is therefore crucial. It is not heroism, and the show does not frame it as ethical clarity. Greg does the dirty work, then keeps something back. He learns that exposure can be turned into leverage if it is preserved in the right form. The scene begins the long education that will carry him through the cruises scandal and into his strange durability beside Tom. Greg cannot command a room. He can remember what others want forgotten, and he can keep proof in reserve.

Tom and Greg mirror each other here. Tom looks to a prenup for protection and discovers that the real contract is social abasement. Greg obeys a dangerous order and discovers that paperwork can become insurance. The characters who will outlast the succession contest are already learning the lesson the Roy children resist: humiliation can be information, and documents can matter more than declarations.

## The Dinner as Audition

The dinner gathers nearly every active pressure into one room and lets the table sort people by reaction. Connor’s ceremonial instincts, Kendall’s anxious recruitment, Shiv’s tactical watchfulness, Roman’s needling presence, Tom’s insecurity, Greg’s absence, Ewan’s refusal, Marcia’s management, and Gerri’s professional attention all coexist under Logan’s gaze. The scene does not need a single formal confrontation to become adversarial. Its pressure comes from accumulation.

Kendall’s effort to recruit support keeps appearing at the edge of the meal. He does not need many scenes of persuasion for the episode to make its point. The essential fact is that he must ask people to believe him while Logan remains nearby, able to make belief dangerous. Kendall needs allies who do not want to be asked in public. The table exposes the fragility of a coalition built from private recognition. Everyone can feel the problem; few want to own the consequence.

The family game gives the title its immediate dramatic use. A memory exercise should create shared rhythm. Instead, it turns into another test of Logan’s condition and everyone else’s nerve. Logan’s faltering matters, but the more revealing action belongs to the watchers. How much do they register? How much do they conceal? When does concern become disloyalty? The family watches Logan fail, then watches itself watching him.

## Logan’s Public Demonstration

Logan’s cranberry-sauce attack on Iverson is the episode’s final escalation because it makes the private case against him publicly usable. The act is ugly in itself: a child becomes the object through which Logan discharges anger and reasserts dominance. The dramatic force lies in the room’s response. Everyone has to decide what the sight now requires.

The cruelty is not incidental bad manners. It demonstrates that Logan still controls the room’s terms of recognition even while looking vulnerable. His body has been weakened by the stroke, and his judgment is under scrutiny, yet his violence does not remove him from the center. It intensifies his centrality. People look toward him, away from him, toward Kendall, toward one another. The room draws even more tightly around Logan at the very moment his unfitness becomes harder to deny.

The scene helps Kendall without making him powerful. Logan has given Kendall evidence. Gerri and others can now treat private concern as something more concrete. But evidence is not authority. Kendall’s challenge gathers force from recoil, embarrassment, and fear, and those are unstable materials. They can move people toward a vote, but they cannot make Kendall into the center Logan has been.

The hour sharpens two long-term truths early. The Roys cannot convert affection into stable support because every gesture has already been contaminated by ranking and fear. And the figures who learn to survive outside the children’s fantasy of inheritance, especially Tom and Greg, are already treating humiliation and paperwork as opportunity.

“I Went to Market” places those truths at a holiday table and lets the room do the work. Kendall thinks he is moving from family feeling into corporate procedure. For the Roys, those rooms were never separate. By the time the can lands, the price of loyalty has changed. What remains uncertain is whether anyone besides Logan can make the room honor the new terms.
