“Nobody Is Ever Missing” argues that the season 1 finale reveals the Roys’ true genius: not winning conflicts cleanly, but turning ceremony, crisis, and even death into material the room can survive. The wedding is not backdrop but the machine that lets corporate war, marital betrayal, and accidental death be absorbed into etiquette, speeches, corridors, and logistics, where every rupture is subordinated to the next required gesture. Kendall’s bear hug becomes less a boardroom contest than a struggle over narrative control, since Logan’s real power is his ability to convert financial threat back into family speech and command the tempo of the day. The episode’s recurring rooms, stairways, bathrooms, and side spaces show how disaster is managed through controlled disappearances and reentries, while Roman’s failed launch, Greg’s courier role, and Connor’s ceremonial emptiness all sharpen the show’s portrait of form outrunning feeling. Shiv’s late confession to Tom shows intimacy after the fact as a procedural cruelty, exposing how marriage becomes a contract whose terms are renegotiated through timing and leverage. Kendall’s car crash and the waiter’s death mark the most radical failure of Roy control, yet even that horror is quickly folded into rescue, concealment, and family narrative, and Logan’s final “number one boy” embrace crystallizes the series’ central grammar: love as leverage, protection as possession, and succession as the inheritance of a method for making the unbearable administrable.
