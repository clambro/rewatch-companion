“Prague” argues that the episode’s canceled bachelor trip is the perfect image for Succession’s closed world: money can simulate escape, but the characters only descend into tighter corridors of humiliation, leverage, and inherited rank. The underground party turns every intended release into a “closed-loop system,” whether Tom recodes disgrace as status, Greg becomes an instrument of Logan’s surveillance, or Roman treats obscenity and access as adulthood. The episode’s key revelation is that the Roy family’s corporate hierarchy already existed in childhood, crystallized in the cage story where Logan named Roman the weaker dog, so Kendall’s anger at Angela’s rejection and his turn toward Sandy and Stewy become less a principled rebellion than an attempt to reverse an older ranking in corporate form. Shiv’s dinner with Logan repeats the same grammar in quieter terms, as paternal intimacy becomes leverage through exposure of her affair with Nate. Across all three siblings, “Prague” shows that Roy life does not open outward into freedom but folds every bid for movement back into the family’s machinery of shame, coercion, and inheritance.
