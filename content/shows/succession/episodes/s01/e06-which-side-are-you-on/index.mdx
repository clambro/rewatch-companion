## Episode Summary

“Which Side Are You On?” centers on Kendall’s board coup against Logan, a no-confidence vote meant to remove him from Waystar. Kendall works the count, Roman tries to bring Lawrence and Vaulter into line, Shiv uses a DC trip with Nate to keep a political route open, and Tom introduces Greg to a higher class of grotesque consumption. Kendall misses the vote after a failed trip to secure Ilona’s support, Roman folds in the room, Ewan backs Logan out of contempt for Kendall, and the motion ends in a tie. Logan keeps control.

## Arithmetic in a Bad Room

Kendall begins with a plausible corporate problem. Logan is medically compromised, erratic in meetings, hostile to governance, and surrounded by executives who understand the risk even when they will not say so plainly. The no-confidence vote gives Kendall a clean instrument for a disorderly situation. He does not need to prove that he is loved. He needs enough directors to decide that Logan has become unsafe for the company.

For much of “Which Side Are You On?”, that seems possible because Kendall treats the coup as arithmetic. He checks names, pressures holdouts, calls in favors, sends Roman to work Lawrence, and flies out to visit Ilona at her hospital bed because one vote can alter the outcome. The episode lets the count look real. Kendall is not hallucinating support. Frank, Gerri, and other board members have reasons to move. Logan’s grip has loosened enough that a formal challenge can be imagined.

Then the arithmetic enters a room Logan occupies.

A private number becomes a public act, and the public act takes place under the gaze of the man being judged. Logan should recuse himself. He does not. Kendall should be present to hold the coalition together. He is stranded outside Manhattan after the airspace shutdown, reduced to a voice on the phone, trying to conduct a mutiny he cannot physically inhabit. The boardroom is not a neutral container for procedure. It has Logan at the table, Roman in the chair, Gerri watching her own exposure, Frank already implicated, and everyone measuring the cost of being seen.

Kendall’s failure is not a simple matter of incompetence. He has identified a real weakness. He has chosen a real mechanism. The plan nearly succeeds. Its collapse hurts because the difference between victory and failure is so small: a grounded helicopter, a late arrival, a family member’s nerve, an uncle’s disgust. Kendall has prepared for votes, but not for the change in temperature once each person must make his or her position legible in Logan’s presence.

Jeremy Strong plays Kendall’s urgency as both executive focus and bodily agitation. He moves through the day with a strained forward lean, speaking as if speed can harden agreement before doubt reenters. At Ilona’s hospital, the setting strips away the smoothness of his campaign. He is asking a sick board member to participate in a high-risk removal while time leaks away elsewhere. His pitch has the right structure, but the visit also exposes how dependent the plot is on fragile human compliance. People must be reached, moved, reassured, and made brave enough to follow through. Kendall can manage the first three tasks better than the fourth.

The episode’s title, taken from the old labor song, asks for side-taking as a public declaration. Here the question has been emptied of solidarity and placed inside corporate self-preservation. A side is a liability. It has to be declared, recorded, remembered, punished. The vote forces people to stop speaking in private dissatisfaction and put their names against Logan Roy. That conversion is the hard part.

## Roman’s Bad Pitch

Roman’s Vaulter scene condenses his entire early-season problem. He understands that corporate language is often ridiculous, so he assumes he can win by sounding as if he is above it. Sent to persuade Lawrence, he tries to perform relaxed digital fluency, talking in forced fragments about “tasty morsels” and “groovy hubs,” as though contempt for old media gives him automatic access to new media’s confidence. The words are bad, but the worse mistake is his assumption that a smirk can replace a pitch.

Lawrence receives him with amused disdain. He does not have to fight Roman because Roman never gives him anything solid enough to fight. The scene’s comedy depends on how quickly Roman’s knowingness turns helpless. He can mock the conventional business meeting, but he cannot produce the thing the meeting still requires: a reason for Lawrence to risk himself. When Lawrence jokes about Roman going “post-literate,” Roman does not control the joke. He becomes its object.

Roman is often the sibling most accurate about the falseness around him. He can smell boredom, fakery, puffed-up seriousness, and family theater. In the Vaulter meeting, that talent yields nothing. He knows enough to avoid sounding like a legacy executive in a suit, but he has no replacement language beyond jittery slang and sexualized insolence. His cynicism cannot be translated into persuasion.

That failure prepares his boardroom abstention without reducing it to cowardice alone. Roman has been useful to the coup as long as the coup remains talk, posture, and secret alignment. He can enjoy the idea of Logan being beaten. He can help Kendall while the consequences remain slightly unreal. Once Logan sits across from him and the vote becomes an act that will be remembered by the father he still wants to survive, Roman loses the thread. His body gets there before his politics do. He does not need to make a pro-Logan argument. He only needs to withhold himself at the decisive second.

The series will return to this Roman pattern with greater cruelty after Logan’s death. He often sees the con before the others, then reaches for the con anyway because it is also the family’s only recognized form of attachment. Here, before Gerri becomes his more serious channel of instruction and before his late attempt to borrow Logan’s brutality, the pattern appears in miniature: diagnose the false ritual, sneer at it, then fail when the ritual asks for a durable position.

## Shiv Keeps Another Door Open

Shiv’s DC subplot is brief, but it should not be mistaken for absence from the succession fight. While Kendall tries to move the board, Shiv tests whether her future can be routed through politics rather than Waystar. Nate offers access, flirtation, professional possibility, and the old charge of a relationship that has not quite stayed buried. Shiv’s scenes with him are light in surface texture, but they are not idle. She is asking what kind of leverage she can hold if she is not sitting inside the family company when the next realignment happens.

Her banter with Nate has the Roy habit of turning attraction into positioning. She does not simply want a job, and she does not simply want an affair. She wants options that do not require her to stand in Logan’s office asking to be chosen. DC gives her a different grammar: campaign discipline, ideological branding, professional intimacy disguised as strategy. It is an arena where she can imagine herself as cleaner, sharper, less dependent on family permission.

Yet the timing binds her to the boardroom she is not in. Kendall’s coup unfolds while Shiv keeps herself mobile, and mobility is her defense. Later, Logan will bring her closer by dangling succession directly, and she will discover how easily her political fluency can be absorbed into the company’s appetite. In “Which Side Are You On?”, that trap has not closed. The DC material catches her at an earlier point, still trying to maintain a path that could make Waystar optional.

The episode places her beside Nate rather than her brothers because Shiv’s relation to the family contest is already indirect. Kendall wants the room. Roman wants the father’s attention without the full burden of action. Shiv wants the ability to choose the room later. Her intelligence often lies in delay, leverage, and exit routes; her weakness lies in overestimating how much freedom those routes provide once Logan notices them.

## Tom and Greg at the Side Table

Tom and Greg supply the episode’s tonal counterpoint, but their material is more than release from the board mechanics. Tom, newly buoyant because the cruises threat seems to have receded, behaves like a man who has mistaken temporary reprieve for safety. His night with Greg turns money into performance: expensive service, forbidden delicacy, the pleasure of teaching someone else how to feel inferior in the correct restaurant.

The ortolan scene, with napkins over heads and shame converted into luxury, gives the episode a smaller, stranger version of its main question. Which side are you on? In Tom’s world, the answer starts with what you will swallow. Greg’s uncertainty, his gauche references, and his inability to know the rules make him available for instruction. Tom’s correction of Greg’s taste, including the California Pizza Kitchen exchange, is funny because the stakes are absurdly low and socially exact. To be close to these people is to be told that your pleasures are wrong, your manners are visible, and your gratitude should be immediate.

The jokes also show how ordinary power talk has already become for the people below the Roy children. Tom can discuss danger, status, and humiliation as dinner conversation. Greg absorbs the lesson unevenly, but he is learning the useful parts: stay near access, let embarrassment pass through you, keep listening. The siblings imagine control as a title waiting at the end of a contest. Tom and Greg are closer to the show’s practical curriculum. They learn who can be mocked, who can be used, and how quickly relief can become another kind of exposure.

Used sparingly, their scenes sharpen rather than distract. Kendall’s coup is high procedure. Tom and Greg show the same world in social miniature, where rank appears as consumption, ridicule, and apprenticeship. The board vote asks directors to risk themselves openly. Tom asks Greg to participate in degradation and call it privilege. Both scenes require the weaker person to decide how much discomfort access is worth.

## Ewan’s Useful Contempt

Ewan’s vote is the coldest complication in the episode because it denies Kendall the satisfaction of automatic anti-Logan feeling. Ewan despises much of what Logan has built. He regards the Roy family as morally and spiritually diseased. Kendall has reason to think that such contempt might be converted into a vote against his father. Instead, Ewan directs his contempt at the coup itself.

His decision helps Logan, but it does not arise from affection or confidence. Ewan does not suddenly approve of his brother’s company. He refuses to let Kendall’s maneuver pass as public virtue. To him, the vote looks like family appetite dressed in procedural language, a son using corporate concern to advance an internal seizure. He may be right about Kendall’s mixture of motives, but the effect of his judgment is brutally practical: Logan survives.

That distinction keeps Ewan from becoming the episode’s moral exit. His opposition to Logan remains real, but it is not cleanly usable by anyone else. Inside this family, even hatred can be folded back into Logan’s advantage if the alternative seems vain, reckless, or insufficiently serious. Ewan’s vote is an act of family contempt that preserves the family patriarch.

The boardroom ending gives that contradiction its physical form. Logan sits where he should not sit. Kendall speaks from where he should not be. Roman cannot bring himself to strike. Ewan refuses Kendall while strengthening Logan. The count lands in a tie, and the failed rebels immediately learn what it means to have made opposition visible without making it successful. Frank is out. The others are marked. Logan does not need a triumphant speech; he needs only the continued right to punish.

“Which Side Are You On?” leaves the Roys with a dry procedural fact that carries more weight than any declaration. A vote was called. The votes were counted. The challenge failed. Logan remains in place because the people against him could not make their opposition sturdier than their fear, disgust, vanity, or hesitation. The Roys keep having to choose sides inside rooms that are already tilted toward Logan.
