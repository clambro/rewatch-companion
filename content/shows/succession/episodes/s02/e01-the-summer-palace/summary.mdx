“The Summer Palace” argues that succession in _Succession_ is not chiefly about inheritance but about atmosphere, placement, and controlled exposure: Logan’s power lies in deciding who hears what, where people stand, and how private knowledge is distributed. Kendall’s televised capitulation turns his ruin into a market signal and proof of paternal command, while the rancid Hamptons house, with dead raccoons in the chimney, functions as the episode’s central image of concealed rot that wealth cannot cleanse. Logan’s discarded meal and pizza replacement show him converting failed ceremony into a new one on his terms, then sorting the family into isolated transactions rather than allowing any genuine collective deliberation. Shiv’s private designation as successor is the episode’s seductive center, but it is also the clearest example of Logan’s method: he flatters her grievance and competence while keeping the promise secret, making her heir only as a managed compartment of his authority. Kendall, meanwhile, is reduced to placed evidence, extracted from rehab, publicly scripted, and later sent alone to warn Sandy and Stewy, so that his apparent agency remains borrowed. Roman’s crisis, Tom’s partial advancement, and Greg’s low-level usefulness all reinforce the same pattern: Logan does not resolve the family’s conflicts, he arranges them inside contamination, preserving control by keeping everyone close, compromised, and unevenly informed.
