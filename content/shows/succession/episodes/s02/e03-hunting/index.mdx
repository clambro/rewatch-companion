## Episode Summary

“Hunting” takes the Roy family and Waystar’s senior staff to a hunting retreat in Hungary while Logan advances a plan to acquire Pierce Global Media. The trip is meant to consolidate support, but it turns into an inquiry after Logan learns that someone has spoken to Michelle Pantsil, the biographer pursuing him, and that the Pierces have already heard about the proposed acquisition.

Greg confesses to Tom that he spoke to Pantsil. Roman uses Tabitha’s connection to Naomi Pierce to help open a channel to PGM. Shiv remains in New York while sending Tom to oppose the deal on her behalf, and Connor releases an anti-tax presidential campaign video despite Shiv’s objections. At dinner, Logan interrupts the meal, interrogates the room, and forces Tom, Greg, and Karl into “Boar on the Floor.” The episode ends after Logan learns that the late Mo was the Pantsil source, Frank reports that PGM is interested in a meeting, and Logan calls Shiv to tell her it is time to bring her into the company.

## The Lodge as Managed Exposure

The hunting lodge looks, at first, like one of Logan’s preferred theaters of masculine restoration: guns, meat, antique rooms, expensive isolation. The setting promises retreat from the corporate floor, but the episode uses that promise against the people gathered there. No one becomes freer outside New York. The distance removes the usual exits. The staff, executives, children, and hangers-on sleep under the same roof, eat in the same rooms, and wait for Logan to decide what counts as loyalty.

The literal hunt barely matters as a test of courage. The danger has been arranged in advance. Guides handle the conditions, and the guests perform a fantasy of hard men returning to older, rougher customs. Men who cannot speak plainly in a boardroom dress themselves in sporting ritual and call it vigor. The boar is less revealing than the staging around it, because the hunt’s controlled violence prepares the indoor violence to come.

The lodge turns leisure into surveillance. Logan’s guests cannot tell when the retreat has stopped being social. A drink, a joke, a side conversation, a phone call, a hesitation at the wrong moment can become evidence. The walls do not close in through physical threat. They close in because Logan keeps changing the status of ordinary behavior. He turns atmosphere into inquiry before anyone can object to the terms.

The PGM bid belongs to the same impulse. Logan’s proposed acquisition is a defensive enlargement of Waystar, a bid to become too large for Sandy and Stewy’s attack and to absorb a rival institution with cultural authority. He has heard that information is moving without him: Pantsil has sources, the Pierces know too much, Roman has opened an unauthorized channel, Shiv has registered opposition from outside the room. Logan does not sit with that uncertainty. He enlarges, presses, summons, hunts.

## The Dinner Table as Interrogation

The dinner begins from a familiar social order and shows Logan dismantling it piece by piece. The table has been prepared for civility. People have places, plates, glasses, courses, the expected language of guests and hosts. Even in this family, a formal meal carries the residue of manners. Logan violates that arrangement without quite abandoning it. He keeps the room, keeps the audience, keeps the seating, and changes the meal’s purpose.

The interruption is the first act of control. Dinner stops being dinner because Logan says it does. Conversation gives way to questioning. The meal becomes a hearing, but without rules stable enough to protect anyone. Logan asks about the Pierce leak and the Pantsil leak as if he is gathering facts, yet the scene has no real evidentiary standard. What he wants is not a clean answer. He wants bodies under pressure. He wants to see who twitches, who flatters, who overexplains, who tries to disappear.

Here humiliation feels administrative rather than spontaneous. Logan’s anger is theatrical, but it is also procedural. He sorts the room. He assigns positions. He identifies suspects. He changes the use of space. The table that moments earlier organized status now organizes exposure: who sits near him, who can be questioned, who must watch, who may be used as an example. No one stands and says that the premise is absurd. No one leaves. Even those who privately know the PGM bid is dangerous remain inside the order he has imposed.

Tom’s role in the dinner begins before he lands on the floor. Shiv has sent him to Hungary with a task he can barely perform: carry her objection to Logan without sounding like its author. His phrasing, with its nervous attempt to avoid ownership of the dissent, captures his whole position. He wants credit for being useful to those below him and safety from those above him. The dinner punishes that middle condition. Tom is too important to be absent and too weak to be protected.

Greg, meanwhile, has already confessed to Tom that he spoke to Pantsil. The confession is not a moral cleansing. It is a bid for shelter. Greg gives Tom information because Tom is the nearest authority who might absorb danger on his behalf. Tom hesitates because the information has value in more than one direction. He can protect Greg, threaten him, trade him, or keep him available. Their scenes sharpen the episode’s account of how knowledge moves through Waystar. It travels sideways between anxious climbers, then downward into people with less room to maneuver.

Tom and Greg are not comic padding here. Their panic supplies a smaller version of the company’s circulation system. Greg leaks, confesses, withholds, and hopes to remain usable. Tom receives the confession and immediately feels the burden of deciding whether intimacy or advantage should govern his next move. The later durability of their cruelty begins in exchanges where protection already contains threat and friendship already sounds like custody.

## The Floor Game

“Boar on the Floor” creates a second dining room beneath the first. Logan orders Tom, Greg, and Karl away from the table and onto the ground. The gesture is blunt, but the scene’s force comes from its exactness. The men keep their suits. They keep their names. They keep their corporate biographies. Then they crawl, oink, and fight over a piece of sausage while their colleagues watch from chairs.

The bodies matter. Tom’s size makes his abasement awkward and exposed. Greg’s long limbs make panic visible before he can organize language around it. Karl, older and supposedly insulated by rank, becomes proof that title does not hold once Logan changes the rules. The forced oinking is not incidental grotesquerie. It replaces testimony with compliance. Logan does not ask them to tell the truth in a form that can be checked. He asks them to submit to a sound. The room learns more from their willingness to perform the command than it could from anything they say.

The sausage gives the ritual its cruel little center. Evidence is displaced by meat. The men lunge for the object because Logan has made it meaningful, not because it contains meaning. The game’s stupidity is part of its discipline. To participate is to accept that nonsense becomes law when Logan administers it. The humiliation is corporate order stripped to its method: identify risk, isolate bodies, extract compliance, record who obeys.

The watchers complete the scene. Gerri stays still because she understands that dissent would only create a new target. Frank, newly returned, is reminded that favor is never a safe condition. Kendall watches with the deadened alertness of someone who has already been broken back into service. Roman jokes, flinches, and keeps trying to remain outside the center until the center finds him.

Then Kendall gets Roman’s phone and exposes the contact with Naomi Pierce. Logan does not detect Roman’s action himself; Kendall, the chastened son, uses the chaos to locate a brother’s vulnerability. Roman has done exactly the kind of thing he imagines Logan wants: he has found an angle, used a sexual-social connection, and tried to help make the Pierce acquisition possible. But he has done it without permission to define the approach.

Logan calling him a moron in front of the room lands because it turns attempted usefulness into public stupidity. Roman’s offense is not simple betrayal. It is misreading. He mistakes Logan’s appetite for authorization. He assumes that wanting what Logan wants will protect him. The insult teaches otherwise. Logan alone gets to decide which actions count as initiative and which count as contamination.

Roman’s face after the insult is more revealing than his jokes before it. He can mock the room’s rituals with unusual accuracy, but he cannot withstand the father’s verdict. The scene starts with Tom, Greg, and Karl on the floor, yet Roman becomes the one most clearly exposed. He remains in his chair, formally above the crawling men, while Logan lowers him by language.

The next morning, Roman needs Gerri to translate the injury into something manageable. He asks how to get Logan’s respect, and Gerri gives him a corporate answer: management training. The advice is funny because it is so inadequate to the wound, but it is also exact. Roman wants recognition as a son. Gerri offers a process for becoming less useless as an executive. She can give him form, procedure, and tactical steadiness. She cannot give him the paternal confirmation he keeps disguising as business ambition.

## Side Rooms and Return Calls

Shiv’s off-site material is not a separate story about freedom. Her distance is temporary. In New York, she tries to stop Connor’s campaign video and moves through her private life as if she can keep the family machine at arm’s length. The affair with the actor belongs to that illusion of separable compartments. Tom is in Hungary carrying risk; Shiv is elsewhere arranging desire under terms she believes she controls.

Connor’s campaign announcement briefly extends the family pattern in another key. His anti-tax platform is less a political program than ceremonial self-authorship. He records himself into seriousness. The consultants, the video, the language of candidacy, the posture of public purpose: all of it tries to make a role harden around him. In Hungary, Logan forces a room to recognize his will through fear. Connor tries to purchase recognition through ceremony. The difference in menace is enormous, but both gestures trust staging to confer importance.

Shiv’s final phone call folds the off-site thread back into the main design. Logan has learned that Mo was the Pantsil source, which lets him close one line of suspicion without admitting how much damage his hunt has done. Frank brings news that PGM may take a meeting, so the reckless enlargement remains alive. Then Logan calls Shiv and tells her it is time to bring her into the company.

Shiv has avoided the lodge, but she has not avoided Logan’s method. He recruits after a night of enforced obedience, when the room has been reminded what proximity costs. The call sounds like elevation, and it is not empty. Logan wants her intelligence, her polish, her usefulness against rivals and siblings. But he offers entry on his schedule, under his terms, after demonstrating elsewhere that every room he controls can become a test without warning. Shiv hears an opening. The rewatching viewer hears the door closing around it.
