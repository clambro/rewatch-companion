“Vaulter” frames Kendall’s forced destruction of his own acquisition as Logan’s deliberate loyalty test, not a mere business correction: Roman supplies the contempt that licenses the shutdown, but Kendall is made to carry out the sacrifice because the real point is to convert his original modernizing fantasy into proof of obedience. The episode treats Vaulter as an object lesson in delegated destruction, where Kendall’s repair instincts, Lawrence’s insight, and the staff’s unionizing open-plan vulnerability are all rendered irrelevant by Logan’s authority. The repeated office scenes turn the same space from collaborative promise into exposure and execution, while Kendall’s hollow performance, his later battery theft, and Logan’s “Make yourself at home” show that he has not regained agency so much as been domesticated by submission. The subplots echo the same arithmetic of disposability: Tom learns managerial cruelty by hunting “50 skulls,” Greg finds shelter only in surplus space he cannot own, and Shiv hears that her supposed succession is really another form of delay. Across these threads the episode sharpens Succession’s vocabulary of reduction, measuring people in headcount, square footage, months, and utility, and showing that the Roy children inherit Logan’s language of cutting before they inherit any real power.
