“Breakage” argues that the meth operation has crossed from improvisation into administration, so the central problem is no longer making product but managing leakage, labor, and retaliation in a world where every container fails. Jesse understands breakage as the unavoidable cost of street distribution, while Walt reads it as an insult to sovereignty, which reveals his demand for total control even as the business scales beyond anything he can personally oversee. The episode’s recurring images of clogged toilets, medical bills, damaged goods, exploded beer bottles, and Tuco’s lucite-encased grill all stage the same idea: pressure cannot be sealed indefinitely, and disposal never fully erases what has been made. Skyler’s forced apology of Marie and Hank’s panic attack after his promotion extend that logic into domestic and institutional life, showing that hidden strain demands admission or erupts as bodily or procedural failure. Walt’s decision to hand Jesse a gun turns a bookkeeping loss into managed violence, confirming that the operation now requires enforcement, not just chemistry, and that scale means other people, other risks, and a more visible form of damage.
