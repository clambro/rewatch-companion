"Peekaboo" argues that Jesse’s role in _Breaking Bad_ is becoming the one who notices the human remainder everyone else steps over, even when that noticing only produces damage control rather than rescue. In the Spooge plot, the episode turns attention, neglect, and visibility into its core vocabulary: the beetle, the abandoned child, the peekaboo game, and the filthy house all dramatize how disappearance can mean abandonment and reappearance can mean threat. Jesse is forced to shift from enforcer to feeder to watcher, and the child’s presence keeps interrupting the criminal role he was sent to perform, making care the burden he inherits. Walt’s parallel story shows the opposite use of concealment: in the polished space of his Gretchen lie, he turns injury into a usable grievance and protects his authority by refusing explanation, using apology and privacy as shields for self-defense. The episode’s contrast between Spooge’s exposed squalor and Walt’s socially maintained fiction shows two modes of harm, one that cannot hide and one that survives by looking respectable. The ATM death and Jesse’s final act of carrying the boy outside complete the hour’s bleak logic, where attention can save someone from the immediate room of harm but cannot restore what neglect and appetite have already destroyed.
