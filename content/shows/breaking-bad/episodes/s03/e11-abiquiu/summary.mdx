“Abiquiu” is organized around repetition as an active trap, where repeated images, repeated labor, and repeated geography reveal that memory and recovery are not escapes from damage but alternate ways it persists. The O’Keeffe museum flashback gives the episode its central vocabulary: Jesse and Jane’s discussion of repeated door paintings frames recurrence as both the desire to preserve feeling and the sign of being stuck inside it. That logic governs Jesse’s storyline, as recovery meetings become a sales space, Andrea and Brock briefly offer the possibility of ordinary connection, and Tomás and Combo’s corner return him to the same criminal terrain in a new form. Hank’s rehabilitation turns recurrence bodily, making progress into humiliating repetition rather than heroic comeback, while Skyler’s move toward the car wash laundering scheme shows domestic competence converting into criminal management and believable continuity. Gus’s dinner with Walt completes the pattern by turning mentorship and hospitality into disciplined control, warning against “the same mistake twice” even as the episode argues that repetition is not a choice characters simply make or avoid but the structure through which work, grief, concealment, and violence keep reappearing.
