“Crawl Space” is about hidden systems ceasing to protect Walt and beginning to expose the family and criminal infrastructure he thought they sustained. The episode’s recurring below-and-behind spaces, the industrial laundry, Ted’s living room, the crawl space under the house, show secrecy as practical architecture that is now failing: Gus’s operation is so well built that Hank’s instinct alone can locate it, Walt’s car crash is a crude last-ditch barrier, and Skyler’s laundering scheme turns from paperwork into bodily injury when administrative pressure requires muscle. At the same time, Jesse’s Mexico cook proves he can function without Walt, Gus’s desert firing reduces Walt from rival to disposable employee, and the threat against Skyler, Walt Jr., and Holly makes “family” the exposed surface of Walt’s criminal life rather than its refuge. The ending in the crawl space crystallizes the episode’s argument: Walt’s buried money, his last fantasy of emergency control and provider authority, is gone, so his laughter reads as the collapse of every role he has used to narrate himself, with the house itself now functioning as a lid over the life he cannot manage.
