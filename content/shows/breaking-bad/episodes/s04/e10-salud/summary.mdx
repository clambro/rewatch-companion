"Salud" argues that control in _Breaking Bad_ is always a performance that depends on ritual, etiquette, and a script other people agree to follow until the body intrudes and breaks the scene open. Gus’s poolside massacre is the episode’s clearest example: he turns hospitality into a killing script by mastering the codes of deference, but the revenge still leaves him vomiting, weakened, and dependent, proving that style and strategy are not the same as invulnerability. Jesse’s Mexico plot gives him real competence through the 96 percent pure cook, yet the episode stresses that skill only increases his value as property, not his agency; he can command a lab but remains embedded in other men’s designs until he acts decisively in the failed escape. The Albuquerque material repeats the same logic in domestic and bureaucratic form: Walt tries to convert physical collapse into emotional honesty with Walt Jr., but the performance exposes his divided paternal economy, while Skyler’s false inheritance for Ted shows that plausible stories cannot force obedience, only invite vanity and misuse. Across all three threads, the episode treats clean surfaces, perfect scripts, and ceremonial gestures as fragile mechanisms that can guide behavior but cannot contain appetite, fear, or consequence.
