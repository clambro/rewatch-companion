“Bullet Points” argues that _Breaking Bad_’s real subject is narration under pressure: who writes the story, who performs it, and who gets reduced to reading evidence in other people’s hands. Skyler’s bullet points turn the gambling lie into a socially usable script, but Walt resents not the deception so much as the shame and lowered status it requires, revealing his need for concealment without self-abasement. That same dynamic runs through Hank’s recovery scenes, where Gale’s notebook, video, and initials make dead authorship circulate as residue and threaten to expose Walt while also tempting him to reclaim credit for the blue meth. Jesse’s neglected house and Mike’s removal of him show Gus’s world treating professionalism as posture, boundary, and maintenance, with Jesse becoming an administrative problem rather than a psychological case. Mike’s ambush, Saul’s disappearer pitch, and the episode’s many lists, files, and notes all reinforce the same point: Walt can survive other people’s scripts only by accepting a smaller, more diminished role than his pride allows, and that humiliation is becoming harder for him to bear.
