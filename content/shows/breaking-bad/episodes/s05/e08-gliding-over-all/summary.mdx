“Gliding Over All” argues that Walt’s empire has reached its smoothest and most frightening form by becoming pure administration: murder, distribution, laundering, and family management are all reduced to workflow, logistics, and maintenance rather than direct confrontation. The episode’s key contrast is between scale and residue, as Walt’s polished system depends on bodies, evidence, cash, and written traces that keep threatening to make consequence legible again. Todd’s blank usefulness replaces Jesse’s volatile conscience and shows how far Walt has moved toward an empire that values compliance over relationship, while Lydia is spared not by mercy but because her logistical value outweighs her danger. The prison montage and the three-month production sequence make killing and labor feel clerical, exposing how “success” has drained the operation of romance and turned mastery into endless upkeep. Skyler’s storage-unit pile of uncountable cash punctures Walt’s provider fantasy by showing that the money is no longer usable enough to justify itself, and Hank’s exhaustion and tree-marking memory frame his investigation as a search for something physical and finite in a world of disappearing evidence. The ending crystallizes the episode’s central idea: Walt can suppress witnesses and restore domestic calm, but he cannot control residue, and the smallest overlooked object, Gale’s inscribed copy of _Leaves of Grass_, defeats his largest administrative victory by turning the home’s most ordinary surface into proof.
