“Felina” is a finale about Walt arranging one last chain of consequences while stripping away every saving myth attached to him, especially the family alibi and the outlaw romance. The episode frames his return as trespass rather than homecoming, reducing his power to logistics, coercion, and delayed access: he routes money through the Schwartzes, gives Skyler evidence instead of repair, and confesses that he did it for himself, because he liked it and felt alive. Jesse’s storyline sharpens the show’s labor politics by contrasting his careful making of a wooden box with his enslavement as a meth worker in Jack’s compound, where Walt’s planned massacre becomes an ugly recognition that Jesse is not a rival but a captive tool. Walt’s final acts, the M60, the ricin in Lydia’s Stevia, the money trust, are all engineered consequences rather than redemptive gestures, and the episode insists that even when Walt frees Jesse and dies in the lab, he does not restore family, morality, or innocence. What remains is the dead syntax of his talent, the blue meth, the body, and the residue of damage.
