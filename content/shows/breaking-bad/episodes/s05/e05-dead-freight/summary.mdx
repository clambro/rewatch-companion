“Dead Freight” treats containment as its governing fantasy: if the vessel is right, the timing exact, and the appearances preserved, then danger can be made to move cleanly without leaving a trace. The freight-train heist, with its hoses, buried tank, and water substitute, turns Walt’s criminal genius into a model of industrialized laundering, where theft becomes a problem of weight, volume, and plausible absence. But the episode insists that perfect procedure only makes the human remainder easier to erase. The same logic governs the domestic plot, where Walt Jr. is rerouted like cargo, Skyler reduces family life to supply routes and cover, and Hank’s office becomes another site for concealed extraction. The cold open’s tarantula in a jar frames the hour’s core insight: transparency is not safety, only a way of seeing control. Todd’s polite wave followed by the killing of the boy completes the episode’s argument that the cleanest systems are the ones most capable of converting a witness into disposable residue, leaving Jesse as the last character able to register the difference between successful execution and moral catastrophe.
