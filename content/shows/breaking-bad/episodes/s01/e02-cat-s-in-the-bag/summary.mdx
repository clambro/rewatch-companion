“Cat’s in the Bag...” argues that _Breaking Bad_’s first real cleanup turns crime into a problem of domestic labor, bad containment, and stubborn residue rather than cinematic mastery. The episode strips Walt and Jesse’s transgression down to materials, rooms, errands, and stains, showing how chemical knowledge collides with ordinary architecture: hydrofluoric acid destroys the bathtub, the floor, and the illusion that a house can absorb what happens inside it. That collapse exposes the series’ early logic that transformation is never erasure, only a shift from one kind of evidence to another. Krazy-8’s captivity pushes the episode from logistics into moral improvisation, with Walt trying to convert a living person into a manageable problem while still preserving a self-image of reluctant necessity. The classroom chiral-molecules scene underscores the split between public clarity and private chaos, as Walt can still perform expertise in one room while his life elsewhere becomes improvised damage control. Skyler’s subplot works as practical investigation rather than intuition alone, using phone records and Jesse’s visible online persona to show how ordinary domestic methods can trace Walt’s hidden life. The ending’s gas mask in the desert completes the episode’s argument that concealment is always provisional: objects survive, circulate, and become evidence, and the first cook leaves behind a trail of exposed things rather than a finished plan.
