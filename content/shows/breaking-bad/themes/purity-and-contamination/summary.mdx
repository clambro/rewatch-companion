The essay argues that purity in _Breaking Bad_ is Walt’s fantasy of separation, a belief that chemical exactness can insulate the cook, the product, and the man from the moral mess they generate. From the pilot’s “no adulterants” ethic and the ritual of stripping off “good clothes,” to the blue meth that makes exactness legible as brand and status, Walt treats cleanliness as proof of mastery and exceptionality. But the series repeatedly shows that purity is not opposed to contamination, it enables it: the superlab’s sealed procedures, Gale’s devotional craftsmanship, and Gus’s disciplined order all make violence more efficient, replaceable, and scalable. “Box Cutter” reveals that immaculate systems absorb blood as part of their logic, while “Fly” turns contamination inward, showing that the true impurity is Walt himself, the withheld act and unprocessed guilt he cannot isolate from the work. By the end, Blue Sky symbolizes not innocence but scalable corruption, as purity becomes the mechanism through which crime is stabilized, commodified, and detached from the bodies it destroys.
